Neuroanatomy IBB 2022 — Comprehensive Quiz

20 questions spanning all major chapters, mapped to Bloom's Taxonomy.

Q1. The central nervous system consists of which two major divisions?

  1. The brain and the cranial nerves
  2. The brain and the spinal cord
  3. The cerebrum and the cerebellum
  4. The forebrain and the brainstem

Answer: B Explanation: The CNS is defined as the brain and spinal cord (B). Cranial nerves are part of the peripheral nervous system. The cerebrum and cerebellum are parts of the brain, but not the full CNS. The forebrain and brainstem omit the spinal cord. Bloom Level: Remember Topic: Introduction to the CNS

Q2. The embryonic brain develops from a strip of ectodermal cells that form the neural _____ , which encloses the neural canal.

Answer: tube Also accept: plate then tube (the neural plate folds to form the neural tube) Bloom Level: Remember Topic: Development of the Nervous System

Q3. The grey matter of the spinal cord can be subdivided into a series of layers called:

  1. Funiculi
  2. Laminae of Rexed
  3. Dorsal columns
  4. Spinal segments

Answer: B Explanation: The laminae of Rexed (B) are the ten layers of spinal grey matter. Funiculi (A) are the white-matter columns. The dorsal columns (C) contain ascending sensory tracts. Spinal segments (D) refer to levels defined by nerve-root emergence, not histological layers. Bloom Level: Remember Topic: Spinal Cord

Q4. Which of the following is NOT one of the three precerebellar nuclei of the hindbrain?

  1. External cuneate nucleus
  2. Inferior olive
  3. Red nucleus
  4. Basilar pontine nuclei

Answer: C Explanation: The three precerebellar nuclei are the external cuneate nucleus, the inferior olive, and the basilar pontine nuclei (A, B, D). The red nucleus (C) is a midbrain nucleus that gives rise to the rubrospinal tract; it is not a precerebellar nucleus. Bloom Level: Understand Topic: Brain Stem

Q5. Damage to the oculomotor nerve (CN III) results in all of the following EXCEPT:

  1. Divergent squint (the eye is pulled laterally and downward)
  2. Ptosis (drooping of the eyelid)
  3. Inability to abduct the eye (lateral gaze palsy)
  4. Dilatation of the pupil

Answer: C Explanation: CN III supplies all extraocular muscles except the superior oblique (CN IV) and lateral rectus (CN VI). Lateral gaze palsy (C) results from damage to the abducens nerve (CN VI), not CN III. Divergent squint occurs because the unopposed lateral rectus pulls the eye laterally. Ptosis results from paralysis of levator palpebrae superioris. Pupil dilatation results from unopposed sympathetic input after loss of the Edinger–Westphal parasympathetic fibres. Bloom Level: Understand Topic: Brain Stem Cranial Nerves

Q6. The cerebellar cortex is made up of three layers. The output cells of the cerebellar cortex, whose axons project to the deep cerebellar nuclei, are the:

  1. Granule cells
  2. Purkinje cells
  3. Climbing fibres from the inferior olive
  4. Mossy fibres from the pontine nuclei

Answer: B Explanation: Purkinje cells (B) are the sole output neurons of the cerebellar cortex; their axons synapse on the deep cerebellar nuclei. Granule cells (A) are tiny interneurons that receive mossy-fibre input and relay to Purkinje dendrites. Climbing fibres (C) and mossy fibres (D) are afferent inputs, not cortical output cells. Bloom Level: Understand Topic: The Cerebellum

Q7. The visual pathway terminates in which nucleus of the thalamus?

  1. Ventroposterior nucleus
  2. Medial geniculate nucleus
  3. Dorsal lateral geniculate nucleus
  4. Ventrolateral nucleus

Answer: C Explanation: The dorsal lateral geniculate nucleus (C) receives optic-tract fibres and projects to primary visual cortex. The ventroposterior nucleus (A) receives somatosensory input. The medial geniculate nucleus (B) is the auditory relay. The ventrolateral nucleus (D) receives cerebellar and pallidal input for motor control. Bloom Level: Understand Topic: Diencephalon

Q8. The suprachiasmatic nucleus of the hypothalamus functions primarily as:

  1. The centre for controlling feeding and drinking behaviour
  2. A 24-hour circadian clock that is adjustable by light information from the optic nerves
  3. The main centre for thermoregulation and body-temperature homeostasis
  4. The source of releasing hormones that control the anterior pituitary

Answer: B Explanation: The suprachiasmatic nucleus (B) sits above the optic chiasm and receives direct retinal input to synchronise its endogenous ~25-hour rhythm to the 24-hour day. Feeding (A) is controlled mainly by the ventromedial and lateral hypothalamic nuclei. Thermoregulation (C) involves the preoptic area. Releasing hormones (D) are produced primarily by the paraventricular nucleus. Bloom Level: Understand Topic: Hypothalamus

Q9. Broca's area, the motor speech area in the dominant hemisphere, corresponds to which Brodmann areas?

  1. Areas 39 and 40
  2. Areas 3, 1, and 2
  3. Areas 45 and 44
  4. Area 17

Answer: C Explanation: Broca's area comprises Brodmann areas 44 and 45 (C) in the inferior frontal gyrus. Areas 39 and 40 (A) form Wernicke's area (speech comprehension). Areas 3, 1, and 2 (B) constitute primary somatosensory cortex. Area 17 (D) is primary visual cortex. Bloom Level: Apply Topic: Telencephalon / Brodmann Areas

Q10. A stroke damaging the internal capsule of the cerebral hemisphere would cause paralysis and muscle stiffness in:

  1. The same side of the body as the lesion
  2. The opposite side of the body
  3. Both sides of the body equally
  4. Only the muscles of the face

Answer: B Explanation: The corticospinal tract crosses to the opposite side at the pyramidal decussation in the caudal hindbrain. A lesion above this decussation (e.g., in the internal capsule) therefore produces contralateral motor deficits (B). The internal capsule is a common site of haemorrhagic stroke in humans. Bloom Level: Apply Topic: Motor Systems

Q11. A patient with a complete transection of the left lateral spinothalamic tract at the T5 spinal level would experience loss of pain and temperature sensation in:

  1. The left lower limb
  2. The right lower limb
  3. Both lower limbs
  4. The right upper and lower limbs

Answer: B Explanation: The lateral spinothalamic tract carries pain and temperature. Second-order neurons cross the midline in the anterior white commissure within a few segments of entry, so the tract runs contralateral to the side of the body it serves. The left lateral spinothalamic tract at T5 carries information from the right lower limb (B). At T5, pain and temperature from the right upper limb would still enter above the lesion and cross, and then join the left tract rostral to the lesion — so only the right lower limb is affected. Bloom Level: Apply Topic: Sensory Systems (Somatosensory)

Q12. A patient with a complete lesion of the right optic tract would most likely experience:

  1. Complete blindness in the right eye
  2. Loss of the left visual field in both eyes (left homonymous hemianopia)
  3. Loss of the right visual field in both eyes (right homonymous hemianopia)
  4. Bitemporal hemianopia (loss of the temporal visual fields)

Answer: B Explanation: The right optic tract carries axons from the left halves of both visual fields (temporal retina of the left eye and nasal retina of the right eye). Damage therefore eliminates vision in the left hemifield of both eyes (B). Bitemporal hemianopia (D) results from a lesion at the optic chiasm. Complete unilateral blindness (A) requires damage to the optic nerve or eye itself. Bloom Level: Apply Topic: Sensory Systems (Visual)

Q13. Which statement correctly distinguishes the sympathetic from the parasympathetic nervous system?

  1. Sympathetic ganglia are located near target organs; parasympathetic ganglia are near the spinal cord
  2. Preganglionic sympathetic neurons are located in the brainstem and sacral spinal cord
  3. Postganglionic sympathetic neurons are noradrenergic; parasympathetic ganglionic neurons are cholinergic
  4. The sympathetic system is responsible for "rest and digest" functions

Answer: C Explanation: Postganglionic sympathetic neurons release noradrenaline (norepinephrine), making them noradrenergic (C). Parasympathetic ganglionic neurons release acetylcholine (cholinergic). Statement A is the reverse of the truth: sympathetic ganglia lie close to the spinal cord, parasympathetic ganglia lie near or within the target organ. Statement B describes the parasympathetic, not sympathetic, outflow. Statement D describes the parasympathetic system. Bloom Level: Apply Topic: The Autonomic Nervous System

Q14. The arterial circle at the base of the brain, formed by the anastomosis of the internal carotid and vertebrobasilar arterial systems, is called the _____ .

Answer: Circle of Willis Also accept: cerebral arterial circle Bloom Level: Apply Topic: Blood Supply to the Brain

Q15. A spinal cord lesion selectively destroys the left gracile fasciculus. Which sensory deficit would this produce?

  1. Loss of pain and temperature sensation in the left leg
  2. Loss of fine touch, vibration, and proprioception in the left leg
  3. Loss of fine touch, vibration, and proprioception in the right leg
  4. Loss of pain and temperature sensation in the right leg

Answer: B Explanation: The gracile fasciculus carries fine touch, vibration, and proprioception from the ipsilateral lower limb. Unlike the spinothalamic tract, the dorsal columns do not cross in the spinal cord — they ascend ipsilaterally and synapse in the gracile nucleus of the medulla, where the second-order neuron then decussates. Therefore, a left gracile lesion (B) spares pain/temperature (which travel in the spinothalamic tract) and does not affect the right side. Bloom Level: Analyze Topic: Spinal Cord / Sensory Pathways

Q16. In a developing embryo, loss of sonic hedgehog (Shh) expression in the notochord would most directly impair which of the following?

  1. The formation of the three primary brain vesicles
  2. The migration and formation of the neural crest
  3. The development of ventral neural-tube structures, including motor neurons
  4. The closure of the rostral neuropore (neural tube closure)

Answer: C Explanation: Shh secreted by the notochord induces ventral patterning of the neural tube, including the formation of the basal plates and motor neurons (C). Dorsal patterning is driven by Wnt and BMP (opposing Shh). Neural crest formation (B) is a dorsal event triggered by BMP/Wnt at the neural-plate margin. Brain-vesicle formation (A) and neural-tube closure (D) are separate processes that depend on other gene cascades. Bloom Level: Analyze Topic: Development of the Nervous System

Q17. A patient with Parkinson's disease presents with resting tremor, rigidity, and difficulty initiating movements (bradykinesia). These symptoms result from degeneration of the dopaminergic pathway originating in the:

  1. Substantia nigra and projecting to the striatum (caudate and putamen)
  2. Ventral tegmental area and projecting to the accumbens nucleus
  3. Motor cortex and descending in the corticospinal tract
  4. Cerebellum and projecting to the ventrolateral thalamus

Answer: A Explanation: Parkinson's disease involves degeneration of the dopaminergic nigrostriatal pathway from the substantia nigra pars compacta to the striatum (A). Dopamine deficiency in the striatum disrupts the balance of direct/indirect basal ganglia pathways, leading to bradykinesia, rigidity, and tremor. The ventral tegmental–accumbens pathway (B) is involved in reward, not movement initiation. Corticospinal damage (C) causes paralysis and spasticity, not the Parkinsonian triad. Cerebellar damage (D) causes ataxia and incoordination, not resting tremor. Bloom Level: Analyze Topic: Motor Systems / Basal Ganglia

Q18. If the cerebral aqueduct is completely blocked, cerebrospinal fluid would accumulate in which of the following?

  1. The fourth ventricle only
  2. The lateral ventricles and the third ventricle
  3. The subarachnoid space only
  4. All four ventricles equally

Answer: B Explanation: CSF flows from the lateral ventricles → third ventricle → cerebral aqueduct → fourth ventricle. A block at the aqueduct prevents CSF from reaching the fourth ventricle, so CSF backs up in the lateral and third ventricles (B), causing hydrocephalus. The fourth ventricle (A) and subarachnoid space (C) would receive less CSF than normal. Bloom Level: Analyze Topic: Circulation of Cerebrospinal Fluid

Q19. A stroke patient can produce fluent but meaningless speech and has great difficulty understanding spoken language. Based on Brodmann's classification, this syndrome most likely results from damage to:

  1. Broca's area (Brodmann areas 44 and 45) in the dominant hemisphere
  2. Wernicke's area (Brodmann areas 39 and 40) in the dominant hemisphere
  3. The primary motor cortex (Brodmann area 4)
  4. The primary auditory cortex (Brodmann area 41)

Answer: B Explanation: Wernicke's area (areas 39 and 40) in the dominant hemisphere is responsible for language comprehension. Damage produces fluent (Wernicke's) aphasia: speech is fluent but empty/meaningless, and comprehension is impaired. Damage to Broca's area (A) produces non-fluent aphasia with preserved comprehension. Damage to area 4 (C) causes contralateral motor weakness without language impairment. Bilateral damage to area 41 (D) causes cortical deafness, not aphasia. Bloom Level: Evaluate Topic: Telencephalon / Brodmann Areas

Q20. A patient presents with excessive urination (polyuria) and excessive thirst (polydipsia) following a severe head injury. Blood tests show very low levels of vasopressin (antidiuretic hormone, ADH).

Q20a. Which hypothalamic nuclei are most likely damaged?
Q20b. How does ADH normally reach the bloodstream from these nuclei?
Q20c. Explain the physiological mechanism by which ADH deficiency causes polyuria.

Model Answer: (a) The supraoptic and paraventricular nuclei of the hypothalamus produce vasopressin (ADH). (b) These nuclei contain large neurosecretory cells whose axons travel through the pituitary stalk to the posterior pituitary. ADH is transported by axonal transport to axon terminals in the posterior pituitary, where it is stored in secretory vesicles and released into the bloodstream upon appropriate neural signals (e.g., increased plasma osmolality or decreased blood volume). (c) ADH acts on the kidneys by increasing the insertion of aquaporin-2 water channels in the collecting duct, which increases water reabsorption and concentrates the urine. Without ADH, the collecting ducts are impermeable to water, so large volumes of dilute urine are produced (polyuria). The resulting fluid loss triggers compensatory thirst (polydipsia).

Key points required:

  • Identification of supraoptic and paraventricular nuclei as the site of ADH production
  • Axonal transport of ADH to the posterior pituitary for storage and release
  • ADH increases water reabsorption in the kidney collecting ducts via aquaporin-2 channels
  • ADH deficiency → inability to concentrate urine → large volumes of dilute urine → compensatory polydipsia

Bloom Level: Evaluate Topic: Hypothalamus / Endocrine Control

Weak Area Diagnostic

After taking this quiz, review the following topics if you answered incorrectly in those areas:

  • Introduction to the CNS (Q1): Re-read the opening chapter on the hierarchical organisation of the brain, spinal cord, and cranial/spinal nerves.
  • Development of the Nervous System (Q2, Q16): Focus on neural-tube formation, dorsoventral patterning by Shh/Wnt/BMP, brain vesicles, and the role of Hox genes in hindbrain segmentation.
  • Spinal Cord (Q3, Q15): Review the laminae of Rexed, the organisation of grey vs. white matter, and the course of the dorsal columns and spinothalamic tracts.
  • Brain Stem (Q4): Study the precerebellar nuclei (external cuneate, inferior olive, basilar pontine) and the difference between hindbrain and midbrain structures.
  • Brain Stem Cranial Nerves (Q5): Revisit the oculomotor nerve: its nuclei (main motor + Edinger–Westphal), muscles supplied, and the full triad of CN III palsy signs.
  • The Cerebellum (Q6): Review the three cortical layers, Purkinje cells as the sole output, and the roles of mossy vs. climbing fibres.
  • Diencephalon (Q7): Study the major thalamic relay nuclei (VP, DLG, MG, VL) and their respective sensory or motor modalities.
  • Hypothalamus (Q8, Q20): Focus on the suprachiasmatic nucleus (circadian clock), the paraventricular and supraoptic nuclei (posterior pituitary), the portal system (anterior pituitary), and survival behaviour control.
  • Telencephalon / Brodmann Areas (Q9, Q19): Review the key Brodmann areas (4, 3-1-2, 17, 41, 44-45, 39-40) and the functional specialisation of major lobes and language areas.
  • Motor Systems (Q10, Q17): Study the corticospinal tract course (internal capsule → pyramid → decussation), the nigrostriatal pathway and Parkinson's disease, and the striatal–pallidal–thalamic motor circuit.
  • Sensory Systems — Somatosensory (Q11): Revisit the three-neuron chain of the spinothalamic tract, its crossing pattern in the spinal cord, and the distinction between lateral (pain/temp) and ventral (touch) tracts.
  • Sensory Systems — Visual (Q12): Study the retinal projection, optic chiasm crossing of nasal fibres, optic-tract organisation, and visual-field defects at different lesion sites.
  • Autonomic Nervous System (Q13): Review the sympathetic chain, craniosacral parasympathetic outflow, the two-neuron chain, and the neurotransmitter differences (cholinergic vs. noradrenergic).
  • Blood Supply to the Brain (Q14): Review the circle of Willis, the territories of the anterior, middle, and posterior cerebral arteries, and the venous sinuses.
  • Circulation of CSF (Q18): Understand the complete CSF pathway: lateral ventricles → third ventricle → aqueduct → fourth ventricle → subarachnoid space → arachnoid granulations.
