Glossary: Neuroanatomy

Based on Neuroanatomy IBB 2022 by Charles Watson, Matthew Kirkcaldie, and Friedrich Schwarz.

1. CNS Development and Organization

  • Central Nervous System (CNS): The brain and spinal cord. The CNS consists of the forebrain (cerebral hemispheres, hypothalamus, diencephalon), brain stem, and spinal cord. Example: All processing of sensory information and motor commands occurs within the CNS.
  • Neural tube: The embryonic precursor to the CNS, formed when the edges of the neural plate curl medially and join. The neural tube encloses the neural canal and gives rise to the brain and spinal cord.
  • Neural crest: Cells that break away from the dorsal side of the closing neural tube and migrate away to form the peripheral nervous system, the melanocytes of the skin, and the bones and muscles of the face.
  • Neuromeres: Segmental subdivisions of the developing brain — five in the forebrain, two in the midbrain, and twelve in the hindbrain. Each neuromere has alar (sensory) and basal (motor) components around the neural canal. Example: The hindbrain's twelve neuromeres consist of the isthmus plus eleven rhombomeres (r1–r11).
  • Sonic hedgehog (Shh): A gene expressed in the notochord that induces the development of ventral structures (such as motor neurons) in the neural tube. → vs Wnt and BMP genes: these dorsally expressed genes direct the alar plates toward sensory functions, creating the dorsoventral patterning of the neural tube.
  • Alar plate: The dorsal region of the neural tube directed by Wnt and BMP genes toward sensory processing functions. Example: The alar plate gives rise to the dorsal horn of the spinal cord which receives sensory input. → vs Basal plate: the ventral region directed by Shh toward motor functions, giving rise to the ventral horn.
  • Cephalic flexure: A nearly 180-degree bend in the neuraxis at the level of the midbrain during development. It pushes the hypothalamus into a ventral position under the diencephalon and historically led to the misconception that the hypothalamus is merely a ventral part of the thalamus.
  • Rhombomeres: The eleven hindbrain segments (r1 through r11), defined by the expression of Hox genes. The dorsal portions of the isthmus and r1 give rise to the cerebellum.
  • Prosomeres (pronunciation: PROH-soh-meers): The three diencephalic segments of the forebrain. Prosomere 1 (p1) contains the pretectal area; prosomere 2 (p2) contains the thalamus; prosomere 3 (p3) contains the prethalamus.
  • Hox genes: Homeobox genes that control hindbrain and spinal cord segmentation. The first four Hox groups (hox1–hox4) are expressed in the hindbrain; hox5–hox13 are expressed in the spinal cord, trunk, limbs, and tail.
  • Neuroplasticity: The ability of the mature brain to change itself by generating new neurons or making new connections. Example: MRI studies of medical students found increased grey matter in parietal cortex and hippocampus during exam preparation.
  • Growth cone: The highly mobile, enlarged tip of a growing axon that "feels" its way through tissue by detecting chemical signals that guide it toward or away from targets. It contains a flattened lamellipodium and fine filopodia that explore the environment.

2. Spinal Cord

  • Spinal cord: A long cylindrical CNS structure extending from the base of the skull to the first lumbar vertebra (L1) in humans. It contains sensory neurons, motor neurons, long tracts to the brain, preganglionic autonomic nuclei, and pattern generators for complex movements like locomotion.
  • Laminae of Rexed (pronunciation: LAM-i-nee of REK-sed): Nine layers of spinal grey matter. Layers 1–4 form the dorsal horn (sensory input); layer 5 contains pattern generators for limb movements; layers 7–9 form the ventral horn (motor functions); layer 9 contains the alpha motor neurons supplying muscles.
  • Grey matter: The central butterfly-shaped area of the spinal cord containing neuron cell bodies, divided into dorsal horns (sensory) and ventral horns (motor). — vs White matter: the surrounding area of myelinated axons divided into dorsal, lateral, and ventral funiculi (columns).
  • Dorsal root ganglion (DRG): A swelling on the dorsal root of each spinal nerve containing the cell bodies of sensory neurons. The central process of each DRG cell enters the spinal cord to synapse with second-order neurons. Example: When you touch a hot surface, the DRG neuron carries the signal from your finger to the spinal cord.
  • Dermatome: A specific strip of skin supplied by a single spinal nerve. Dermatomes extend from the midline of the back around to the ventral midline. Example: The C5 dermatome covers the lateral side of the shoulder and arm.
  • Corticospinal tract: The major descending motor pathway from the primary motor cortex (Brodmann area 4) to spinal motor neurons. It travels through the internal capsule, cerebral peduncle, basilar pons, and medullary pyramid, then most fibers cross at the pyramidal decussation. Damage causes paralysis and stiffness on the opposite side of the body.
  • Pyramidal decussation (pronunciation: dee-kuh-SAY-shun): The crossing of corticospinal fibers at the caudal hindbrain, causing motor cortex fibers to control the opposite side of the body. Example: A stroke in the left motor cortex causes right-sided paralysis because the corticospinal fibers have crossed.
  • Spinothalamic tract: An ascending sensory pathway for pain and temperature. First-order neurons in the dorsal root ganglia synapse in the substantia gelatinosa; second-order axons cross in the anterior white commissure and ascend to the ventroposterior thalamus. → vs Dorsal column–medial lemniscus pathway: the parallel pathway for fine touch, proprioception, and vibration, which does not cross until the hindbrain.
  • Nucleus of Clarke: A prominent cell group (dorsal nucleus) found in thoracic and upper lumbar spinal cord levels, giving rise to the dorsal spinocerebellar tract that conveys proprioception to the cerebellum of the same side.
  • Pattern generators: Neural circuits in the spinal cord (primarily lamina 5) that can initiate coordinated movements such as locomotion, posture, and withdrawal reflexes without input from the brain. Example: The leg withdrawal reflex when stepping on glass is orchestrated entirely by spinal pattern generators before the brain registers pain.
  • Brachial plexus: A network formed by the C5, C6, C7, C8, and T1 spinal nerves that supplies the upper limb muscles. — vs Lumbosacral plexus: formed by L2, L3, L4, L5, and S1 spinal nerves supplying the lower limb muscles.

3. Brain Stem

  • Brain stem: Made up of the midbrain (mesencephalon) and hindbrain (rhombencephalon). It controls breathing, chewing, swallowing, vocalisation, facial expression, eye movements, and alertness, and acts as a corridor for tracts between the forebrain and spinal cord. Example: The brain stem centers for breathing keep us alive without conscious effort.
  • Midbrain (pronunciation: mes-en-SEF-uh-lon): The rostral part of the brain stem, continuous with the forebrain. Its dorsal surface bears two pairs of bumps — the superior colliculi (visual) and inferior colliculi (auditory).
  • Medial lemniscus (pronunciation: lem-NIS-kus): A compact bundle of crossed sensory fibers formed after the gracile and cuneate nuclei relay touch and proprioception information. It ascends through the brain stem to synapse in the ventroposterior nucleus of the thalamus.
  • Periaqueductal gray (PAG): The pale grey matter surrounding the cerebral aqueduct in the midbrain, involved in pain modulation and autonomic control.
  • Substantia nigra (pronunciation: sub-STAN-shee-uh NY-gruh): A dark midbrain region stained by iron deposits in fresh specimens. Its dopamine-containing neurons project to the striatum via the nigrostriatal pathway; damage to this projection underlies Parkinson's disease. Example: The progressive loss of substantia nigra dopamine neurons causes the tremor, stiffness, and difficulty initiating movement typical of Parkinson's disease.
  • Red nucleus: A large midbrain cell group that gives rise to the rubrospinal tract, a descending pathway controlling flexor movements of the limbs.
  • Cerebral peduncle (crus cerebri): A large fiber bundle at the lateral edge of the midbrain containing corticospinal and corticopontine fibers descending from the cerebral cortex toward the hindbrain.
  • Reticular formation: A network of brain stem centers that maintain alertness, regulate autonomic functions (breathing, cardiovascular control), and modulate spinal cord activity. Example: The reticular formation keeps the forebrain awake and alert; damage can cause coma.
  • Inferior olive: A large folded nucleus in the rostral half of the caudal hindbrain that provides timing signals for movement coordination. Its axons cross the midline and reach the cerebellum via climbing fibers, firing at about 10 Hz.
  • Basilar pontine nuclei: The largest group of neurons in the human brain stem. They receive input from the cerebral cortex and send axons across the midline via the middle cerebellar peduncle to the opposite cerebellar hemisphere, forming the massive pontine bulge visible on the ventral hindbrain.
  • Fourth ventricle: The cavity of the hindbrain formed when the neural canal opens dorsally. CSF flows from the aqueduct into the fourth ventricle and escapes through holes in its roof to reach the subarachnoid space.

4. Cranial Nerves (III–XII)

  • Oculomotor nerve (CN III): Supplies all the eye muscles except the superior oblique and lateral rectus. It also contains parasympathetic fibers (Edinger–Westphal nucleus) that constrict the pupil and control the ciliary muscle. Damage causes divergent squint, ptosis (drooping eyelid), and dilated pupil.
  • Trochlear nerve (CN IV) (pronunciation: TROK-lee-ar): The only cranial nerve to exit the dorsal surface of the brain stem. It supplies the superior oblique muscle of the eye. Its nucleus lies in the isthmus (most rostral hindbrain segment).
  • Trigeminal nerve (CN V) (pronunciation: try-JEM-i-nal): The largest cranial nerve, divided into a large sensory root (supplying the skin of the face) and a smaller motor root (supplying the muscles of mastication). Its sensory nuclei include the principal sensory nucleus, the spinal trigeminal nucleus, and the mesencephalic nucleus.
  • Abducens nerve (CN VI): Supplies the lateral rectus muscle of the eye. Its nucleus lies next to the midline near the medial longitudinal fasciculus in the hindbrain.
  • Facial nerve (CN VII): Supplies the muscles of facial expression, taste fibers to the anterior two-thirds of the tongue, and parasympathetic fibers to the submandibular and sublingual salivary glands. Its motor fibers take an unusual course by looping around the abducens nucleus before exiting.
  • Vestibulocochlear nerve (CN VIII): Receives sensory fibers from the semicircular canals (balance) and the cochlea (hearing). It enters the brain stem as two bundles: vestibular fibers ventral to the inferior cerebellar peduncle and cochlear fibers dorsal and lateral to it.
  • Glossopharyngeal nerve (CN IX): Receives taste fibers from the posterior third of the tongue, supplies parasympathetic fibers to the parotid salivary gland, and innervates the palatoglossus muscle via the nucleus ambiguus.
  • Vagus nerve (CN X) (pronunciation: VAY-gus): Supplies parasympathetic fibers to the thoracic and abdominal viscera (heart, lungs, stomach, intestines). Its dorsal motor nucleus lies in the floor of the fourth ventricle.
  • Accessory nerve (CN XI): Has a cranial part arising from the nucleus ambiguus (contributing to pharyngeal supply) and a spinal root arising from C2–C4 cervical motor neurons supplying the sternomastoid and trapezius muscles.
  • Hypoglossal nerve (CN XII): Supplies the muscles of the tongue. Its nucleus lies medial to the dorsal motor nucleus of the vagus, and its fibers emerge between the inferior olive and the pyramid.
  • Nucleus of the solitary tract: A hindbrain sensory nucleus receiving taste fibers from the facial, glossopharyngeal, and vagus nerves, as well as general visceral afferent fibers. It projects to the ventroposterior thalamus, which then sends taste information to the insular cortex. Example: The solitary tract stands out in myelin-stained sections as a dark bundle of fibers among the cranial nerve nuclei.
  • Edinger–Westphal nucleus: The small parasympathetic component of the oculomotor complex that controls pupillary constriction and accommodation (ciliary muscle).

5. Cerebellum

  • Cerebellum (pronunciation: ser-uh-BEL-um): An outgrowth of the rostral hindbrain (isthmus and rhombomere 1) responsible for coordinating complex movements, posture, and balance. It contains about 80% of all brain neurons — mostly tiny granule cells. Example: Damage to one cerebellar hemisphere causes incoordination and tremor on the same side of the body.
  • Vermis (pronunciation: VER-mis): The midline portion of the cerebellum divided into ten identifiable lobules (1–10) by deep fissures across all mammals. The primary fissure separates anterior lobules (1–5) from posterior lobules (6–10); lobule 10 is called the nodule.
  • Purkinje cells (pronunciation: pur-KIN-jee): The large output neurons of the cerebellar cortex, forming a single cell layer between the molecular and granule cell layers. Their axons project to the deep cerebellar nuclei. Example: Each Purkinje cell has an elaborate fan-like dendritic tree extending into the molecular layer.
  • Granule cells: The most numerous neurons in the brain (about 70% of all neurons), packed at about one million per cubic millimeter in the cerebellar granule cell layer. They receive input from mossy fibers and send axons to Purkinje cell dendrites.
  • Mossy fibers: Afferent projections to the cerebellar cortex that synapse on granule cells. They arise from the pontine nuclei, other precerebellar nuclei in the hindbrain, and the spinal cord. — vs Climbing fibers: direct projections from the inferior olive to Purkinje cell dendrites that control the timing code of learned movements.
  • Dentate nucleus: The largest of the three deep cerebellar nuclei on each side, laterally placed within the deep cerebellar white matter. It is a key output relay for cerebellar coordination signals bound for the thalamus.
  • Middle cerebellar peduncle (pronunciation: pe-DUNG-kul): The huge fiber bundle connecting the basilar pontine nuclei to the opposite cerebellar hemisphere. It is the largest of the three cerebellar peduncles. → see Inferior cerebellar peduncle: carries spinocerebellar and olivocerebellar fibers into the cerebellum. → see Superior cerebellar peduncle: carries cerebellar output toward the thalamus, decussating in the isthmus.
  • Precerebellar nuclei: Three large hindbrain nuclei projecting to the cerebellum — the inferior olive, the basilar pontine nuclei, and the external cuneate nucleus. Example: The external cuneate nucleus sends proprioceptive information from the upper limb and neck to the cerebellum of the same side.
  • Cerebellar cortex layers: Three uniform layers found throughout the cerebellum — the outer molecular layer (containing Purkinje dendrites and parallel fibers), the Purkinje cell layer, and the inner granule cell layer. Deep to these lies the deep cerebellar white matter.

6. Diencephalon and Hypothalamus

  • Diencephalon (pronunciation: dy-en-SEF-uh-lon): "The interbrain" — sits between the midbrain and the hypothalamus. It consists of three prosomeres: the thalamus (p2), the prethalamus (p3), and the pretectal area (p1).
  • Thalamus (pronunciation: THAL-uh-mus): The central part of the diencephalon; the major sensory relay station for all sensory systems except olfaction. Key nuclei include the ventroposterior (somatosensory), dorsal lateral geniculate (vision), medial geniculate (hearing), and ventrolateral (motor coordination). Example: The ventroposterior nucleus receives touch information from the medial lemniscus and projects to the postcentral gyrus.
  • Ventroposterior nucleus (VP): The thalamic somatosensory relay nucleus receiving input from the medial lemniscus and spinothalamic tracts and projecting to the primary somatosensory cortex (Brodmann areas 3, 1, and 2).
  • Dorsal lateral geniculate nucleus (DLG): The thalamic visual relay nucleus receiving input from the optic tract and projecting to the primary visual cortex (V1, Brodmann area 17). — vs Medial geniculate nucleus (MG): the thalamic auditory relay nucleus receiving input from the inferior colliculus and projecting to the primary auditory cortex (A1, Brodmann area 41).
  • Ventrolateral nucleus (VL): The thalamic nucleus that receives input from the cerebellum (via the superior cerebellar peduncle) and the globus pallidus, projecting to the primary motor cortex to coordinate voluntary movement.
  • Zona incerta (pronunciation: ZOH-nuh in-SER-tuh): A region in the prethalamus (p3) important for motor control. It is a target of deep brain stimulation (DBS) for reducing tremor and muscle stiffness in Parkinson's disease.
  • Pretectal area (pronunciation: pree-TEK-tal): The most caudal diencephalic prosomere (p1) containing nuclei for visual reflexes and the posterior commissure.
  • Pineal gland: A gland growing from the dorsal thalamus (p2) that secretes melatonin to influence the sleep–wake cycle.
  • Hypothalamus (pronunciation: hy-po-THAL-uh-mus): The most rostral part of the neural tube, forming two segments (terminal and peduncular). It controls survival behaviors (eating, drinking, reproduction, defense) and directly or indirectly controls the pituitary gland. Example: The suprachiasmatic nucleus above the optic chiasm serves as the brain's 24-hour clock, adjustable by light from the optic nerves.
  • Suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN): The hypothalamic nucleus serving as the circadian clock. Its neurons have an internal timing system that is adjusted by light intensity information from the optic nerves.
  • Paraventricular nucleus (Pa): A hypothalamic nucleus containing large neurosecretory cells that produce oxytocin (uterine contraction, milk secretion, pair-bonding) and vasopressin/antidiuretic hormone (ADH) for release via the posterior pituitary. It also secretes releasing hormones via the portal blood system to control the anterior pituitary.
  • Mammillary body (pronunciation: MAM-i-lair-ee): A caudal hypothalamic structure involved in exploratory and foraging behaviors. It is part of a system including the substantia nigra and ventral tegmental area that initiates orienting, walking, and exploration.
  • Pituitary gland: The endocrine gland controlled by the hypothalamus. The posterior pituitary releases oxytocin and ADH directly from hypothalamic neuron terminals; the anterior pituitary releases growth hormone (GH), thyroid-stimulating hormone (TSH), adrenocorticotrophic hormone (ACTH), follicle-stimulating hormone (FSH), prolactin (PRL), and luteinizing hormone (LH) in response to hypothalamic releasing hormones delivered via a specialized portal blood system.

7. Telencephalon and Cerebral Cortex

  • Telencephalon (pronunciation: tel-en-SEF-uh-lon): The two cerebral hemispheres arising from the alar plate of the rostral hypothalamic segment. Each hemisphere consists of an outer pallium (cortex) and inner subpallium (striatum, pallidum, amygdala, septum).
  • Neocortex: The six-layered cerebral cortex derived from the dorsal pallium. It is massively expanded in humans with folding into gyri (ridges) and sulci (grooves). The six layers are numbered I to VI; primary sensory areas have prominent layers II and IV (granular), while motor cortex (area 4) lacks layer IV (agranular cortex).
  • Hippocampus (pronunciation: hip-oh-KAM-pus): The part of the cerebral cortex derived from the medial pallium, involved in short-term memory registration. It consists of the dentate gyrus, CA3 region, CA1 region, subiculum, and entorhinal cortex. Example: The entorhinal cortex acts as a GPS, containing a precise two-dimensional grid map of surroundings, linking memories to places.
  • Striatum: A subpallial motor control structure consisting of the caudate nucleus and the putamen. It receives input from the motor cortex and the substantia nigra (dopamine) and projects to the pallidum. Example: The dopaminergic nigrostriatal pathway is damaged in Parkinson's disease, disrupting movement initiation.
  • Globus pallidus (pallidum) (pronunciation: GLO-bus PAL-i-dus): A subpallial structure medial to the putamen, forming part of the motor control circuit. It receives from the striatum and projects to the ventrolateral thalamus and the midbrain, using the inhibitory neurotransmitter GABA.
  • Accumbens nucleus (pronunciation: uh-KUM-benz): The ventral part of the striatum that receives a dopaminergic projection from the ventral tegmental area, forming the basis of the brain's internal reward system.
  • Amygdala (pronunciation: uh-MIG-duh-luh): A complex of subnuclei deep to the piriform cortex in the temporal lobe. Its medial group connects with the olfactory system; its lateral group mediates fear responses, emotional memory, and social hierarchy behavior. Example: Unconscious emotional memories formed through the amygdala are very difficult to suppress or erase.
  • Corpus callosum: The largest forebrain commissure, connecting neocortical areas of the two hemispheres. It has a rostral genu (forming the forceps minor to the frontal lobe), a central body, and a thickened caudal splenium (forming the forceps major to the occipital lobe).
  • Fornix (pronunciation: FOR-niks): The large fiber bundle carrying hippocampal output from the subiculum to the septum and hypothalamus.
  • Internal capsule: A strip of white matter in the cerebral hemisphere carrying corticospinal, corticobulbar, and corticopontine fibers. It is a common site of stroke — damage causes paralysis and stiffness on the opposite side of the body.
  • Brodmann area 4 (M1): The primary motor cortex located in the precentral gyrus. It has a prominent pyramidal cell layer (layer 5) but lacks a distinct layer 4 (agranular cortex). Electrical stimulation produces movements on the opposite side of the body, and body parts are represented upside down (somatotopic organization).
  • Brodmann areas 3, 1, and 2 (S1): The primary somatosensory cortex in the postcentral gyrus, receiving touch, proprioception, and pain information from the ventroposterior thalamus. The body is represented topically with the head at the bottom and the lower limb at the top.
  • Brodmann area 17 (V1): The primary visual cortex located on the medial surface of the occipital lobe along the calcarine sulcus. It receives visual information from the dorsal lateral geniculate nucleus. → see Brodmann area 41 (A1): the primary auditory cortex on the superior temporal gyrus inside the lateral fissure.
  • Broca's area (areas 44 and 45): The motor speech area in the inferior frontal gyrus of the dominant hemisphere. — vs Wernicke's area (areas 39 and 40): the speech comprehension area on the superior temporal gyrus caudal to A1 in the dominant hemisphere.

8. Sensory Systems and Motor Pathways

  • Dorsal column–medial lemniscus pathway: The main pathway for fine touch, proprioception, and vibration. First-order neurons in dorsal root ganglia send axons via the gracile (lower limb) and cuneate (upper limb) fasciculi to synapse in the gracile and cuneate nuclei of the hindbrain. Second-order axons cross as internal arcuate fibers and ascend as the medial lemniscus to the ventroposterior thalamus.
  • Lateral spinothalamic tract: The ascending pathway for pain and temperature. First-order neurons synapse in the substantia gelatinosa; second-order axons cross in the anterior white commissure and ascend in the lateral spinal column to the ventroposterior thalamus. → vs Ventral spinothalamic tract: conveys touch sensation.
  • Rods: Retinal photoreceptors sensitive to dim light that do not discriminate color but react quickly to movement. The human retina contains about 700 million rod cells, concentrated outside the macula. — vs Cones: about 7 million cone cells concentrated in the macula, responsible for high-acuity color vision, sensitive to red, green, or blue light.
  • Optic chiasm (pronunciation: KI-az-um): The structure where axons from the nasal (medial) half of each retina cross to the opposite side while temporal (lateral) retinal axons remain uncrossed. This ensures that the right visual cortex receives information from the left visual field of both eyes.
  • Organ of Corti (pronunciation: KOR-tee): The auditory receptor apparatus inside the scala media of the cochlea, sitting on the basilar membrane. Hair cells in the organ of Corti detect sound vibrations and are tonotopically organized — high frequencies at the base, low frequencies at the apex.
  • Semicircular canals: Three fluid-filled (endolymph) canals oriented in different spatial planes (horizontal, sagittal, coronal) that detect head movement. Each canal has an ampulla containing hair cells embedded in a cupula; fluid motion bends the cupula and generates electrical signals. — vs Saccule and utricle: the "static" vestibular system; otolith-containing jelly plates (otolith membrane) detect stationary head position under gravity, set at right angles to each other.
  • Nystagmus (pronunciation: nis-TAG-mus): Involuntary flicking of the eyes from side to side following sustained rotation, caused by continued endolymph movement in the semicircular canals after the head stops moving.
  • Solitary tract: A distinct bundle of taste fibers (from facial and glossopharyngeal nerves) in the hindbrain lying between the dorsal motor nucleus of the vagus and the spinal vestibular nucleus. It is surrounded by the nucleus of the solitary tract.
  • Alpha motor neuron: The large motor neurons in the ventral horn (lamina 9) that directly activate skeletal muscle fibers. Charles Sherrington coined the term "final common pathway" for alpha motor neurons, since all motor control systems ultimately act through them. Example: One alpha motor neuron and all the muscle fibers it innervates constitute a motor unit.
  • Rubrospinal tract: A descending motor pathway from the red nucleus in the midbrain to the spinal cord, involved in controlling flexor movements of the distal limbs. → see Vestibulospinal tracts: descending pathways controlling posture and extensor muscles.

9. Autonomic Nervous System, Blood Supply, CSF, and Staining

  • Sympathetic nervous system: The "fight or flight" division of the autonomic nervous system originating from T1–L2 spinal cord segments. Preganglionic neurons have short axons to sympathetic chain ganglia; postganglionic neurons are noradrenergic and distribute to all body levels. Example: The sympathetic system increases heart rate, dilates airways, and shuts down digestion during stress.
  • Parasympathetic nervous system: The "rest and digest" division originating from the brain stem (via cranial nerves III, VII, IX, and X) and the sacral spinal cord (S2–S4). Preganglionic axons are long; ganglia lie near or within target organs. It lowers heart rate, stimulates salivation and digestion, and controls bladder emptying and penile erection.
  • Enteric nervous system (ENS): The intrinsic neural network embedded in the gut wall containing 200–600 million neurons — more than the entire spinal cord. It controls gut contractions and secretions independently of the CNS through its own reflex systems, using transmitters such as acetylcholine, VIP, nitric oxide, GABA, and serotonin.
  • Circle of Willis: The arterial circle at the base of the brain formed by the two posterior cerebral arteries, the middle and anterior cerebral arteries (from the internal carotids), and the posterior and anterior communicating arteries. It connects the carotid and basilar arterial systems.
  • Middle cerebral artery (MCA): The largest cerebral artery, supplying most of the lateral surface of the cerebral cortex. It is a common site of stroke; the deep perforating branches supply the internal capsule. Example: An MCA stroke can cause contralateral paralysis and loss of speech if Broca's or Wernicke's area is affected.
  • Cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) (pronunciation: seh-REE-bro-SPY-nal): A clear fluid filling the subarachnoid space and ventricles, acting as a shock absorber for the brain. It is produced by the choroid plexus in the lateral, third, and fourth ventricles, flows through the aqueduct, and is absorbed into the venous sinuses via arachnoid granulations.
  • Choroid plexus (pronunciation: KOR-oyd PLEK-sus): The CSF-producing tissue located in the medial wall of the lateral ventricles, the roof of the third ventricle, and the roof of the fourth ventricle. Example: If CSF circulation is blocked, the ventricles expand and compress the cortex, a condition called hydrocephalus.
  • Blood–brain barrier (BBB): A protective system created by membrane specializations in the endothelial cells of brain capillaries. Water-soluble molecules cannot pass through; small lipid-soluble molecules can. The BBB is absent in the circumventricular organs, allowing neurons there to monitor blood chemicals.
  • Nissl stain: A classic histological stain using dyes such as thionin or cresyl violet to reveal the cell bodies and nuclei of neurons and glia. It stains the rough endoplasmic reticulum (Nissl substance) intensely. Example: Nissl stains show the layers of the cerebellar cortex but cannot distinguish between small neurons and glia.
  • Golgi stain (pronunciation: GOL-jee): A silver stain developed by Camillo Golgi that reveals the complete morphology of individual neurons (cell body, dendrites, and axon) by staining only about 1% of neurons in a section. It revolutionized neuroanatomy when used by Ramon y Cajal.
  • Immunohistochemistry (IHC): A staining technique using antibodies to detect specific proteins in neurons. Example: Antibodies against calbindin, calretinin, and parvalbumin are used to identify distinct cell groups; NeuN antibody marks nearly all neurons but never glia.
  • Myelin stain (Weigert or Luxol fast blue): Histological stains that reveal myelinated axon tracts. In brain stem sections, large tracts like the pyramid and medial lemniscus stain darkly while cellular areas like the inferior olive remain pale.
