The Brain’s Hidden Majority: What 86 Billion Neurons Can Teach Us About Mind and Disease

Introduction

Here is a surprising fact about your brain: it contains roughly 86 billion neurons, yet almost 80 percent of them are not in your cerebral cortex — the wrinkled surface we usually picture when we think of the brain. Instead, they are tiny granule cells packed inside the cerebellum, a cauliflower-shaped structure tucked beneath the cerebral hemispheres. These minuscule cells are the unsung heroes of coordination, helping you walk, type, and play a musical instrument without conscious effort.

The brain is even more crowded than it first appears. For every neuron, there are roughly ten glial cells — the so-called “support cells” that do far more than hold neurons in place. They recycle neurotransmitters, maintain the chemical environment, insulate axons with myelin, and even modulate synaptic connections in adulthood. Together, neurons and glia form a dynamic partnership that makes thought, movement, and sensation possible. In this post, we will explore the architecture of the nervous system, from the microscopic wiring of individual cells to the clinical conditions that reveal how the brain works by showing us what happens when it breaks down.

The Cellular Cast: Neurons, Glia, and the Need for Speed

A neuron is essentially an information highway with three main parts: the cell body (soma), branching dendrites that receive signals, and a single axon that transmits electrical impulses to the next cell in the circuit. Dendrites often carry small protrusions called dendritic spines, which expand the surface area available for synaptic contact — the junction where one neuron communicates with another.

Axons come in two varieties: myelinated and non-myelinated. Myelinated axons are wrapped in a fatty insulating sheath that acts like the rubber coating on an electrical wire, preventing signal loss and dramatically increasing conduction speed. In the peripheral nervous system, Schwann cells (also called lemmocytes) produce this myelin, wrapping individually around each axon like a spiral of tape. In the central nervous system, oligodendrocytes perform the same role, but each one can myelinate multiple axons simultaneously. When this insulation breaks down — as it does in multiple sclerosis — nerve impulses can slow to a tenth of their normal speed or stop entirely.

The other glial players are equally vital. Astrocytes are star-shaped cells that absorb excess potassium, recycle neurotransmitters, and secrete growth factors that keep neurons healthy. Microglia serve as the brain’s resident immune cells, responding to injury and infection. Ependymal cells line the fluid-filled ventricles and the central canal of the spinal cord. Because glial cells can divide, they are also the source of primary brain tumors such as glioblastomas when that division becomes uncontrolled.

From Spine to Brain: A Hierarchy of Control

The central nervous system is organized as a functional hierarchy. At the base is the spinal cord, which handles reflexes and automatic movement patterns. When you step on something sharp, your spinal cord pulls your foot away before your brain even registers the pain. It also activates the opposite limb to keep you upright — all without conscious thought. This is modular control: pre-wired circuits that coordinate dozens of muscles as a single functional unit.

Ascending through the brainstem, we find the midbrain and hindbrain, which manage survival functions like breathing, chewing, swallowing, and balance. The brainstem also acts as a corridor for long pathways connecting the spinal cord to the forebrain. Ten pairs of cranial nerves arise from the brainstem, controlling eye movements, facial expression, hearing, and the muscles of the throat and tongue.

At the top of the hierarchy sits the forebrain, home to the cerebral cortex — the seat of sensation, action, thinking, memory, communication, and emotion. The cortex is divided into four lobes (frontal, parietal, temporal, and occipital) and contains roughly 180 distinct functional areas. Beneath the cortex lie the basal ganglia, including the striatum and pallidum, which help select and shape movement patterns. The hypothalamus, positioned below the thalamus, controls survival behaviors such as eating, drinking, reproduction, and defense. It also regulates the pituitary gland, the “leader of the endocrine orchestra,” via both direct neural connections and a specialized portal blood system.

The thalamus sits at the center of this system, receiving sensory input from touch, vision, and hearing and relaying it to the appropriate cortical areas. It is the brain’s central switching station — with one notable exception: smell bypasses the thalamus entirely on its way to the olfactory cortex.

When Wiring Fails: Lessons from Neurology

Neurological disease offers a window into normal brain function. Consider stroke: when a blood vessel supplying the brain is blocked or ruptures, neurons deprived of oxygen and glucose begin to die within minutes. The symptoms depend entirely on which artery is affected. A stroke in the internal capsule — a dense white-matter highway connecting cortex to spinal cord — can cause paralysis and stiffness on the opposite side of the body. A stroke in the speech-dominant hemisphere (usually the left) can rob a person of the ability to speak or understand language. This is why neurologists say “time is brain”: every minute of delayed treatment means more lost neurons.

Demyelinating diseases tell us how critical myelin is for normal function. In multiple sclerosis, the immune system mistakenly attacks oligodendrocytes and myelin sheaths, producing scars called plaques. Without myelin, action potentials must be generated continuously along the axon rather than jumping between nodes — a process called saltatory conduction — which dramatically increases energy demand and causes the profound fatigue typical of MS patients.

Neurodegenerative diseases show us what happens when specific cell populations die. In Parkinson’s disease, dopamine-producing neurons in the substantia nigra degenerate, disrupting a circuit that normally enables smooth movement initiation. The result is tremor, rigidity, and difficulty starting movements. In Alzheimer’s disease, the hippocampus — the brain region that registers new memories — is the first to suffer, which is why patients often forget recent conversations while retaining memories from decades past.

The Brain That Rewires Itself

For much of the twentieth century, scientists believed the adult brain was fixed and unchangeable. We now know this is false. The brain forms an estimated one million new synaptic connections per minute throughout life, a capacity called neural plasticity. After injury, the brain can compensate by rerouting connections and strengthening alternative pathways — the basis of modern rehabilitation after stroke and trauma.

This plasticity is most dramatic during development. The fetal brain generates up to 250,000 new neurons per minute during peak periods. Axons grow toward their targets using molecular signposts — netrins, semaphorins, and ephrins — that attract or repel the growing tip, called the growth cone. Not all connections survive: millions of synapses are pruned during childhood to refine neural circuits, and the same pruning continues at a slower pace throughout life.

The development of the cerebellum illustrates this beautifully. At birth, the cerebellum is tiny, but it grows rapidly during the first six years of life — precisely when children are mastering walking, running, and fine hand coordination. Each folium (the leaf-like folds of the cerebellum) may code for a specific movement sequence. Given the tens of thousands of folia in the human cerebellum, there may even be a “backhand folium” in keen tennis players — a testament to the brain’s lifelong capacity to learn.

Key Takeaways

  • The cerebellum contains the brain’s hidden majority: nearly 80% of all neurons are tiny granule cells that coordinate movement with extraordinary precision.
  • Glial cells outnumber neurons ten to one and perform essential functions including myelination, neurotransmitter recycling, immune defense, and synaptic modulation.
  • The nervous system is organized hierarchically: the spinal cord handles reflexes, the brainstem manages survival functions, and the forebrain supports sensation, thought, and memory.
  • Myelin is critical for neural speed and efficiency: demyelinating diseases like multiple sclerosis demonstrate that without this insulation, nerve conduction slows dramatically or fails entirely.
  • The brain is plastic throughout life: it continually forms new synapses, prunes old ones, and can partially recover from injury by rewiring its connections.
