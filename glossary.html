Glossary: IB Neuroscience

Cells & Tissue

  • Neuron: The structural and functional unit of the nervous system, specialized to conduct electrical signals. Neurons cannot divide (unlike glial cells). Example: Motor neurons in the spinal cord ventral horn.

  • Neuroglia (Glial cells): Specialized non-neuronal supportive cells that provide structural integrity and metabolic support for neurons. They outnumber neurons 10:1. → See Astrocyte, Oligodendrocyte, Microglia, Schwann cell, Ependymal cell

  • Astrocyte: Star-shaped glial cell of the CNS that actively recycles neurotransmitters, secretes neurotrophic factors, dictates synapse number, modulates synapses in the adult brain, maintains ionic composition of extracellular fluid, and contributes to the blood-brain barrier. Example: Reactive astrocytes upregulate GFAP after injury. → vs Oligodendrocyte: Astrocytes are star-shaped and modulate the neuronal environment; oligodendrocytes are smaller with condensed nuclei and form myelin.

  • Oligodendrocyte: The CNS analog of the Schwann cell; forms myelin sheaths around brain and spinal cord axons. A single oligodendrocyte myelinates multiple axons. Example: Target of autoimmune attack in multiple sclerosis. → vs Schwann cell: Oligodendrocytes are in the CNS and myelinate multiple axons; Schwann cells are in the PNS and myelinate one axon.

  • Microglia: The smallest and rarest glial cell; the intrinsic immune effector cells of the CNS. They mediate inflammation following damage or microbial invasion. Derived from bone marrow/monocytes (unlike other glial cells which derive from neural tube). Example: Activated in neurodegenerative diseases.

  • Schwann cell (Lemmocyte): Glial cells of the PNS that wrap individually around peripheral axons to form myelin sheaths. One Schwann cell myelinates one internodal segment of one axon. Example: Peripheral neuropathies often involve Schwann cells.

  • Ependymal cell (Ependyma): Epithelial-like cells that line the ventricles of the brain and the central canal of the spinal cord; involved in CSF production and circulation.

  • Satellite cell: Glial cells that surround each neuron in peripheral ganglia (sensory, sympathetic); provide nutrients and structural support.

  • Nissl body: Chromatophilic granules in the neuronal soma formed by rough endoplasmic reticulum (RER) and polyribosomes; stains basophilically. Named after Franz Nissl. Example: Prominent in spinal cord motor neurons.

  • Neuropil: The region of nerve fibers (axons and dendrites) with numerous synapses and glia, with few neural cell bodies; located in gray matter.

Neuron Structure & Function

  • Cell body (Soma): The expanded portion of the neuron containing the nucleus and most organelles; the main synthetic and trophic center. Stains basophilically due to Nissl bodies.
  • Dendrite: One to many extensions of the cell body specialized to receive input from other neurons or receptors; contain Nissl bodies in proximal parts. Example: Pyramidal cell apical dendrite.
  • Dendritic spine: Small protrusions on dendrites that expand surface area and serve as sites of synaptic contact. Critical for neural plasticity.
  • Axon: Typically one per neuron; specialized for conducting electrical impulses (action potentials). Lacks Nissl bodies and does not stain with routine histological stains. Example: Corticospinal tract axons descend from motor cortex.
  • Axon hillock: The cone-shaped region of the cell body where the axon originates; the site of action potential initiation.
  • Telodendrite: The terminal branching of an axon that forms synaptic contacts with other neurons.
  • Synapse: The specialized point of contact where a neuron communicates with another neuron or effector cell. Composed of presynaptic terminal, synaptic cleft, and postsynaptic membrane. → See Electrical synapse, Chemical synapse
  • Electrical synapse: Two neurons physically and electrically coupled by gap junctions; allows rapid propagation of action potentials directly. Rare in mammals.
  • Chemical synapse: Involves release of neurotransmitter from presynaptic terminal across synaptic cleft to bind receptors on postsynaptic membrane. Most common type.
  • Synaptic vesicle: Small membrane-bound sac in the presynaptic terminal containing neurotransmitter molecules.
  • Postsynaptic thickening: Dense specialization on the postsynaptic membrane visible under electron microscopy.
  • Voltage-gated ion channel: Membrane protein that opens in response to changes in membrane potential; essential for action potential generation and conduction.
  • Ligand-gated ion channel: Receptor that opens an ion channel when a neurotransmitter or other chemical binds to it.
  • G protein-coupled receptor (GPCR): Receptor that activates intracellular G proteins upon ligand binding, leading to second messenger cascades rather than direct ion channel opening.

Neuron Classification

  • Unipolar (Pseudounipolar) neuron: Has one process that bifurcates; cell bodies found in spinal and cranial ganglia. Example: Dorsal root ganglion cells.
  • Bipolar neuron: Has two processes; relatively rare. Example: Retina, certain cranial ganglia.
  • Multipolar neuron: Many processes (typically 1 axon and 2+ dendrites); the most common type. Example: Pyramidal cells, motor neurons.
  • Motor (Efferent) neuron: Innervates muscle or glands; activation leads to motor events. → See Lower motor neuron, Upper motor neuron
  • Sensory (Afferent) neuron: Transfers sensory information (pain, touch, pressure); cell bodies in dorsal root ganglia.
  • Interneuron: Neither motor nor sensory; connects other neurons. Example: Spinal reflex interneurons.
  • Pyramidal cell: The prototype cerebral neuron with large triangular cell bodies; present in cerebral cortex and hippocampus. Example: Betz cells in motor cortex.
  • Purkinje cell: Large distinct neurons in the cerebellum with prominent cell bodies and extensive dendritic trees; the only output neurons of the cerebellar cortex.
  • Stellate cell: GABAergic inhibitory interneurons that control Purkinje cell activity in the cerebellum.

Myelination & Conduction

  • Myelin: A lipid-rich sheath formed by glial cell membranes wrapped around axons; serves as an electrical insulator that dramatically speeds action potential conduction.
  • Myelin sheath: The multilamellar membranous wrapping around axons. In PNS, formed by Schwann cells; in CNS, formed by oligodendrocytes.
  • Node of Ranvier: Regular gaps in the myelin sheath containing exposed Na+ ion channels; sites of membrane depolarization that enable saltatory conduction. Named after Louis Antoine Ranvier.
  • Saltatory conduction: The “jumping” of action potentials from one node of Ranvier to the next; makes myelinated axons conduct up to 100× faster than unmyelinated axons.
  • Internode: The myelinated segment between two nodes of Ranvier.
  • Schmidt-Lanterman cleft (incisure): Remnants of Schwann cell cytoplasm forming funnel-shaped discontinuities in peripheral myelin; not usually present in CNS.
  • Neurokeratin: The protein network remaining after myelin has been removed from a nerve sheath; seen in histological sections.
  • Demyelination: Loss of myelin from axons, causing slowed or blocked nerve impulse transmission. Example: Multiple sclerosis (CNS), Guillain-Barré syndrome (PNS).

CNS vs PNS Organization

  • Central Nervous System (CNS): Consists of the brain and spinal cord.

  • Peripheral Nervous System (PNS): Consists of all cranial and spinal nerves, their roots and ganglia.

  • Nucleus (neuroanatomical): A collection of neuronal cell bodies in the CNS (accumulation of gray matter). Example: Facial nerve nucleus in the pons. → vs Ganglion: A collection of neuronal cell bodies in the PNS. Example: Dorsal root ganglion.

  • Tract: A bundle of axons (nerve fibers) within the CNS; lacks connective tissue sheaths. Example: Corticospinal tract. → vs Nerve: A bundle of axons extending from the brain or spinal cord, surrounded by connective tissue sheaths. Example: Sciatic nerve.

  • Gray matter: Regions containing neuronal cell bodies, dendrites, unmyelinated axons, and synapses; appears gray due to lack of myelin. → vs White matter: Regions containing myelinated axon bundles; appears white due to myelin lipid content.

  • Funiculus: A large bundle of nerve fibers (white matter) in the spinal cord. The spinal cord has three: dorsal, lateral, and ventral funiculi.

  • Fasciculus: A smaller bundle of nerve fibers within a funiculus.

  • Decussation: The crossing of nerve fibers from one side of the CNS to the other. Example: Pyramidal decussation, sensory decussation.

Spinal Cord

  • Spinal cord: The caudal continuation of the medulla oblongata; extends from foramen magnum to approximately L1-L2 in adults. Contains central gray matter (butterfly/H-shaped) and peripheral white matter.
  • Ventral horn (Anterior horn): The ventral region of spinal gray matter containing motor neuron cell bodies.
  • Dorsal horn (Posterior horn): The dorsal region of spinal gray matter receiving sensory input.
  • Intermediolateral horn: The lateral projection of gray matter in thoracic and upper lumbar segments (T1-L2) containing preganglionic sympathetic neurons.
  • Central canal: The fluid-filled channel running through the spinal cord; lined by ependymal cells and containing CSF.
  • Dorsal root: The sensory root of a spinal nerve; carries afferent fibers into the spinal cord. Contains the dorsal root ganglion.
  • Dorsal root ganglion (Spinal ganglion): A ganglion on the dorsal root containing cell bodies of sensory (pseudounipolar) neurons.
  • Ventral root: The motor root of a spinal nerve; carries efferent fibers from spinal cord motor neurons.
  • Rexed’s laminae: Ten histological layers (I-X) of the spinal gray matter defined by cytoarchitecture. Example: Lamina IX contains motor neuron pools.
  • Conus medullaris: The tapered, conical end of the spinal cord at L1-L2.
  • Cauda equina: The bundle of lumbar and sacral nerve roots that descend below the conus medullaris before exiting the vertebral canal.
  • Filum terminale: A thin strand of pia mater extending from the conus medullaris to anchor the spinal cord to the coccyx.

Brain Regions

  • Brainstem: Connects spinal cord with forebrain; consists of midbrain and hindbrain. Contains vital centers for breathing, chewing, swallowing, and cardiovascular control.
  • Midbrain (Mesencephalon): The rostral part of the brainstem containing the superior and inferior colliculi, red nucleus, substantia nigra, and cerebral peduncles.
  • Hindbrain (Rhombencephalon): The caudal part of the brainstem consisting of pons and medulla oblongata; gives rise to the cerebellum dorsally.
  • Pons: The bulging upper part of the hindbrain; contains pontine nuclei and serves as a relay between cerebellum and cerebrum.
  • Medulla oblongata: The caudal part of the hindbrain continuous with the spinal cord; contains vital centers for breathing, heart rate, and blood pressure.
  • Cerebellum: Grows from the dorsal hindbrain; coordinates movement, balance, and posture. Contains ~80% of all neurons in the brain (mostly granule cells). Has three lobes: anterior, posterior, and flocculonodular.
  • Vermis: The midline structure of the cerebellum connecting the two hemispheres.
  • Diencephalon: The central part of the forebrain consisting of thalamus, hypothalamus, subthalamus, and epithalamus.
  • Thalamus: The major relay station for sensory pathways; receives input from all senses except smell and projects to the cerebral cortex. Divided into multiple nuclei.
  • Hypothalamus: The ventral part of the diencephalon controlling survival functions: eating, drinking, thermoregulation, sleep, reproduction, and endocrine function via the pituitary.
  • Pituitary gland (Hypophysis): The “master gland” connected to the hypothalamus by the pituitary stalk; releases hormones regulating growth, metabolism, reproduction, and stress.
  • Subthalamic nucleus: Part of the subthalamus; involved in motor control via connections with basal ganglia. Lesions cause hemiballismus.
  • Epithalamus: Contains the pineal gland (melatonin secretion) and habenular nuclei.
  • Telencephalon: The most rostral part of the brain; consists of the cerebral hemispheres (cortex, basal ganglia, hippocampus, amygdala).
  • Cerebral cortex: The outer gray matter layer of the cerebral hemispheres; undergoes massive expansion and folding (gyri and sulci) in humans. Contains ~180 distinct areas.
  • Gyri (singular: Gyrus): The ridges of folded cerebral cortex.
  • Sulci (singular: Sulcus): The grooves or fissures between gyri.
  • Basal ganglia: Deep gray matter nuclei in the telencephalon including striatum (caudate + putamen), globus pallidus, substantia nigra, and subthalamic nucleus. Critical for motor control.
  • Striatum: The caudate nucleus and putamen together; the main input structure of the basal ganglia.
  • Hippocampus: A curved structure in the medial temporal lobe essential for memory formation and spatial navigation. Subdivided into CA1, CA2, CA3, and dentate gyrus.
  • Dentate gyrus: The input region of the hippocampus; receives information from entorhinal cortex.
  • Amygdala: Almond-shaped nucleus in the temporal lobe involved in emotional processing, especially fear.
  • Corpus callosum: The massive white matter tract connecting the two cerebral hemispheres; allows interhemispheric communication.
  • Internal capsule: A V-shaped white matter structure containing major ascending and descending pathways (corticospinal tract, thalamocortical fibers); common site of stroke.
  • Lateral ventricle: The C-shaped cavity within each cerebral hemisphere containing CSF; connected to the third ventricle via the interventricular foramen.
  • Choroid plexus: The site of CSF secretion into the ventricles; formed by pia mater, ependyma, and blood vessels.

Cranial Nerves

  • Olfactory nerve (CN I): Sensory; carries smell information from olfactory epithelium to olfactory bulb.
  • Optic nerve (CN II): Sensory; carries visual information from retina to optic chiasm.
  • Oculomotor nerve (CN III): Motor; innervates most extraocular muscles (superior, inferior, medial rectus; inferior oblique) and the pupillary constrictor.
  • Trochlear nerve (CN IV): Motor; innervates the superior oblique muscle.
  • Trigeminal nerve (CN V): Mixed; carries sensation from the face and motor innervation to muscles of mastication. Has ophthalmic (V1), maxillary (V2), and mandibular (V3) divisions.
  • Abducens nerve (CN VI): Motor; innervates the lateral rectus muscle.
  • Facial nerve (CN VII): Mixed; motor to facial expression muscles, taste from anterior two-thirds of tongue, parasympathetic to lacrimal and salivary glands.
  • Vestibulocochlear nerve (CN VIII): Sensory; carries hearing (cochlear division) and balance (vestibular division) information.
  • Glossopharyngeal nerve (CN IX): Mixed; taste from posterior one-third of tongue, motor to stylopharyngeus, parasympathetic to parotid gland.
  • Vagus nerve (CN X): Mixed; the most widely distributed cranial nerve; parasympathetic innervation to thoracic and abdominal viscera, motor to pharynx/larynx, sensory from viscera.
  • Accessory nerve (CN XI): Motor; innervates sternocleidomastoid and trapezius muscles.
  • Hypoglossal nerve (CN XII): Motor; innervates tongue muscles.

Development

  • Neural plate: A strip of ectodermal cells that forms the earliest embryonic nervous tissue.
  • Neural tube: Formed by curling and fusion of the neural plate; gives rise to the entire CNS (brain and spinal cord).
  • Neural crest: Cells that break away from the dorsal neural tube; give rise to the PNS (sensory and autonomic neurons, Schwann cells, satellite cells), melanocytes, and facial bones/muscles.
  • Neural ectoderm: The embryonic tissue layer that gives rise to the neural plate and subsequently the neural tube.
  • Sonic hedgehog (Shh): A ventralizing morphogen that directs cells ventral to the neural canal to form basal plates (motor functions).
  • Wnt and BMP genes: Dorsalizing signals that direct cells dorsal to the neural canal to form alar plates (sensory functions).
  • Otx2: A gene expressed in the forebrain and midbrain; defines the forebrain-midbrain boundary.
  • Gbx2: A gene expressed in the rostral hindbrain; defines the midbrain-hindbrain boundary (isthmus).
  • Hox genes: Segmentation genes that pattern the hindbrain and spinal cord along the rostrocaudal axis.
  • Neuromere: A transverse segment of the developing neural tube defined by specific gene expression patterns. The forebrain has 5, midbrain 2, hindbrain 12.
  • Rhombomere: A neuromere in the hindbrain; 11 rhombomeres plus the isthmus.
  • Isthmus: The segment at the midbrain-hindbrain boundary; critical for organizing cerebellar development.
  • Rhombic lip: The dorsolateral region of the hindbrain that gives rise to the cerebellum and precerebellar nuclei.
  • Alar plate: The dorsal half of the developing neural tube; gives rise to sensory interneurons.
  • Basal plate: The ventral half of the developing neural tube; gives rise to motor neurons.
  • Cephalic flexure: The bend in the neural tube at the midbrain level.
  • Progressive myelination: The process by which axons become myelinated after birth; proceeds from spinal cord toward brain and from sensory toward motor pathways.

Sensory Systems

  • First-order neuron: The sensory neuron carrying information from receptor to CNS (cell body in dorsal root ganglion or cranial nerve ganglion).
  • Second-order neuron: The neuron receiving input from first-order neurons and projecting to thalamus (or equivalent relay).
  • Third-order neuron: The neuron projecting from thalamus to sensory cortex.
  • Dorsal column-medial lemniscus pathway: The pathway for fine touch, vibration, and proprioception; ascends ipsilaterally in dorsal columns, synapses in nucleus gracilis/cuneatus, decussates as internal arcuate fibers, and ascends to thalamus (VPL).
  • Spinothalamic tract: The pathway for pain and temperature; crosses in spinal cord ventral white commissure and ascends to thalamus.
  • Lateral geniculate nucleus (LGN): The thalamic relay nucleus for vision; receives input from optic tract and projects to primary visual cortex (V1).
  • Optic chiasm: The X-shaped structure where optic nerve fibers partially cross; nasal retina fibers cross, temporal retina fibers remain ipsilateral.
  • Primary visual cortex (V1, striate cortex): Located in occipital lobe; receives input from LGN. Lesions cause contralateral homonymous hemianopia.
  • Superior colliculus: Midbrain visual center involved in orienting responses and eye movements.
  • Inferior colliculus: Midbrain auditory center; major relay in the ascending auditory pathway.
  • Medial geniculate nucleus (MGN): The thalamic relay nucleus for hearing; projects to primary auditory cortex (A1).
  • Cochlea: The snail-shaped organ of the inner ear containing the organ of Corti with hair cells that transduce sound vibrations into neural signals.
  • Organ of Corti: The sensory epithelium of the cochlea containing hair cells (inner and outer) that detect sound.
  • Spiral ganglion: The ganglion in the cochlea containing cell bodies of bipolar neurons that receive input from hair cells.
  • Semicircular canals: Three fluid-filled loops in the inner ear detecting rotational head movements.
  • Vestibular nuclei: Brainstem nuclei receiving input from vestibular nerve; mediate balance, eye movements, and posture.
  • Olfactory bulb: The rostral forebrain structure receiving input from olfactory nerve; first processing station for smell.
  • Primary olfactory cortex (Piriform cortex): The cortical area receiving direct input from olfactory bulb (unlike other senses, smell bypasses thalamus for initial cortical processing).
  • Nucleus solitarius: The brainstem nucleus receiving taste input from cranial nerves VII, IX, and X; projects to thalamus (VPM).

Motor Systems

  • Corticospinal tract (Pyramidal tract): The major descending motor pathway from cerebral cortex to spinal cord; controls voluntary movement, especially fine finger movements. ~75% decussates at pyramidal decussation.
  • Pyramidal decussation: The crossing of corticospinal fibers in the caudal medulla.
  • Basal ganglia circuits: Subcortical loops (cortex → striatum → pallidum/substantia nigra → thalamus → cortex) that facilitate desired movements and suppress unwanted movements.
  • Substantia nigra: Midbrain nucleus with dopaminergic neurons; degeneration causes Parkinson’s disease. Has pars compacta (dopamine production) and pars reticulata (output to thalamus).
  • Cerebellar peduncles: Three large white matter tracts connecting cerebellum to brainstem: superior (output), middle (input from pons), and inferior (input/output with medulla/spinal cord).
  • Red nucleus: Midbrain nucleus giving rise to the rubrospinal tract; involved in motor control, especially of the upper limbs.
  • Reticular formation: A network of neurons throughout the brainstem core; involved in consciousness, sleep-wake cycles, and motor control.
  • Pontine nuclei: Neurons in the basal pons that relay cortical input to the contralateral cerebellum via the middle cerebellar peduncle.
  • Inferior olive: A wrinkled nucleus in the medulla; sends climbing fibers to cerebellum critical for motor learning.
  • Extrapyramidal system: Motor pathways outside the corticospinal tract, including basal ganglia, cerebellum, reticular formation, and associated tracts.
  • Premotor cortex: Cortical area anterior to primary motor cortex; plans and sequences movements.
  • Supplementary motor area (SMA): Medial premotor cortex involved in planning sequences of movements and bimanual coordination.
  • Primary motor cortex (M1): Located in precentral gyrus (Brodmann area 4); contains Betz cells (giant pyramidal neurons) and has somatotopic organization (motor homunculus).

Autonomic Nervous System

  • Autonomic Nervous System (ANS): The involuntary, two-neuron visceral efferent system innervating cardiac muscle, smooth muscle, and glands.
  • Sympathetic division: Thoracolumbar outflow (T1-L2); prepares body for “fight or flight.” Preganglionic neurons in intermediolateral horn → sympathetic chain or prevertebral ganglia → postganglionic neurons.
  • Parasympathetic division: Craniosacral outflow (CN III, VII, IX, X and S2-S4); promotes “rest and digest.” Preganglionic neurons are long, postganglionic are short (near or in target organ).
  • Enteric nervous system: The “brain in the gut” — an independent network of neurons in the gastrointestinal tract wall capable of local reflexes without CNS input.
  • Sympathetic chain (Trunk): A paired chain of ganglia alongside the vertebral column where sympathetic preganglionic neurons may synapse.
  • Preganglionic neuron: The first neuron in the ANS pathway; cell body in CNS, axon exits via ventral root or cranial nerve.
  • Postganglionic neuron: The second neuron in the ANS pathway; cell body in ganglion, axon innervates effector.
  • Acetylcholine (ACh): The neurotransmitter released by all preganglionic autonomic neurons and all parasympathetic postganglionic neurons.
  • Norepinephrine (Noradrenaline): The neurotransmitter released by most sympathetic postganglionic neurons (except sweat glands which use ACh).

Blood Supply & CSF

  • Circle of Willis: The anastomotic ring of arteries at the base of the brain connecting internal carotid and vertebral basilar systems; provides collateral circulation.
  • Internal carotid artery: Supplies the anterior circulation (anterior and middle cerebral arteries).
  • Vertebral artery: Paired arteries ascending through cervical vertebrae; unite to form the basilar artery.
  • Basilar artery: Formed by union of vertebral arteries; supplies pons and gives rise to posterior cerebral arteries.
  • Anterior cerebral artery (ACA): Supplies medial frontal and parietal lobes; occlusion causes leg weakness.
  • Middle cerebral artery (MCA): The largest cerebral artery; supplies lateral frontal, parietal, and temporal lobes; most common site of stroke (causes face/arm weakness and aphasia).
  • Posterior cerebral artery (PCA): Supplies occipital lobe and inferior temporal lobe; occlusion causes contralateral homonymous hemianopia.
  • Anterior communicating artery: Connects the two anterior cerebral arteries; common site of aneurysm.
  • Posterior communicating artery: Connects internal carotid to posterior cerebral artery; common site of aneurysm causing oculomotor nerve palsy.
  • Cerebrospinal fluid (CSF): Clear fluid filling ventricles and subarachnoid space; cushions the brain, removes waste, and provides buoyancy.
  • Choroid plexus: The site of CSF production in the ventricles; formed by pia mater and ependymal cells with rich capillary networks.
  • Blood-brain barrier (BBB): The selective barrier formed by tight junctions between capillary endothelial cells, basement membrane, and astrocytic end-feet; restricts passage of substances into the brain.
  • Superior sagittal sinus: The major dural venous sinus running along the superior margin of the falx cerebri; site of CSF reabsorption via arachnoid granulations.
  • Meninges: The three connective tissue layers surrounding CNS: dura mater (outer, tough), arachnoid mater (middle), and pia mater (inner, adherent to brain).
  • Epidural space: The potential space between dura mater and skull; site of epidural hematoma.
  • Subdural space: The potential space between dura and arachnoid; site of subdural hematoma.
  • Subarachnoid space: The space between arachnoid and pia containing CSF and major blood vessels.
  • Arachnoid granulations (villi): Projections of arachnoid into dural venous sinuses where CSF is reabsorbed into blood.

Clinical Terms & Disorders

  • Upper motor neuron (UMN) lesion: Damage to corticospinal tract neurons or their axons; causes spastic paralysis, increased tone, hyperreflexia, and Babinski sign. Note: The term “UMN” is anatomically misleading; prefer “corticospinal tract lesion.” → vs Lower motor neuron (LMN) lesion: Damage to motor neurons or their axons; causes flaccid paralysis, decreased tone, hyporeflexia/areflexia, fasciculations, and muscle atrophy.

  • Spastic paralysis: Increased muscle tone with velocity-dependent resistance, hyperreflexia, and clasp-knife phenomenon; caused by corticospinal tract damage.

  • Flaccid paralysis: Loss of muscle tone (hypotonia), diminished or absent reflexes, and muscle atrophy; caused by motor neuron or peripheral nerve damage.

  • Paresis: Partial paralysis; weakness without complete loss of movement.

  • Paralysis: Complete loss of motor function.

  • Babinski sign: Extension (dorsiflexion) of the big toe and fanning of other toes upon stroking the sole; indicates corticospinal tract damage in adults.

  • Tremor: Rhythmic oscillation caused by alternating activity of agonist and antagonist muscles.

  • Resting tremor: Present at rest, decreases with voluntary movement; characteristic of Parkinson’s disease (substantia nigra degeneration).

  • Action tremor: Absent at rest, increases with movement; seen in cerebellar disease, essential tremor, and multiple sclerosis.

  • Ataxia: Incoordination of movement; classic sign of cerebellar damage. Patient’s hand moves unaimed toward target (dysmetria).

  • Chorea: Brief, irregular, dance-like movements; seen in Huntington’s disease.

  • Athetosis: Slow, writhing movements; seen in Huntington’s disease and cerebral palsy.

  • Tic: Involuntary, rapid, repetitive muscle spasms; seen in Tourette’s syndrome and Huntington’s disease.

  • Fasciculation: Visible, brief twitching of muscle fibers caused by spontaneous firing of a motor unit; sign of LMN pathology.

  • Stroke: Acute disruption of blood supply to the brain causing neurological deficit. Types: ischemic (clot) and hemorrhagic (bleed).

  • Multiple sclerosis (MS): Autoimmune demyelinating disease of the CNS causing relapsing-remitting neurological deficits.

  • Parkinson’s disease: Neurodegenerative disease caused by loss of dopaminergic neurons in substantia nigra; characterized by resting tremor, rigidity, bradykinesia, and postural instability.

  • Alzheimer’s disease: Neurodegenerative disease causing progressive dementia; characterized by cortical atrophy, amyloid plaques, and neurofibrillary tangles. Hippocampal involvement causes early memory loss.

  • Amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS): Degeneration of both upper and lower motor neurons causing progressive weakness; also called Lou Gehrig’s disease.

  • Poliomyelitis: Viral infection destroying motor neurons in spinal cord and brainstem; causes flaccid paralysis (pure LMN disease).

  • Epilepsy: Recurrent unprovoked seizures caused by abnormal synchronous neuronal discharges in the brain.

  • Traumatic brain injury (TBI): Brain damage caused by external mechanical force; ranges from concussion to severe diffuse axonal injury.

  • Spinal shock: Temporary loss of reflexes and autonomic function below a spinal cord injury; occurs immediately after trauma.

  • Hydrocephalus: Abnormal accumulation of CSF in ventricles causing ventricular enlargement and increased intracranial pressure.

  • Meningitis: Inflammation of the meninges; can be bacterial (severe), viral, or fungal.

  • Encephalitis: Inflammation of the brain parenchyma, usually due to viral infection.

  • Peripheral neuropathy: Damage to peripheral nerves causing sensory loss, weakness, and/or pain in a glove-and-stocking distribution.

  • Guillain-Barré syndrome: Acute inflammatory demyelinating polyradiculoneuropathy of the PNS causing ascending flaccid paralysis.

  • Rett syndrome: Genetic neurodevelopmental disorder (X-linked, MECP2 mutation) causing severe intellectual disability, loss of motor skills, and characteristic hand-wringing.

  • Neurofibromatosis: Genetic disorder (NF1/NF2 mutations) causing tumors of peripheral nerves (neurofibromas) and other nervous system lesions.

  • Huntington’s disease: Autosomal dominant genetic disorder causing degeneration of striatum; characterized by chorea, athetosis, and dementia.

  • Duchenne muscular dystrophy (DMD): X-linked genetic disorder causing progressive muscle degeneration due to dystrophin deficiency; not a primary neurological disorder but causes secondary motor impairment.

Diagnostic Methods

  • Computed tomography (CT): X-ray-based imaging; excellent for detecting acute hemorrhage, skull fractures, and hydrocephalus.
  • Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI): Uses magnetic fields and radio waves; superior for visualizing soft tissues, white matter lesions, tumors, and posterior fossa structures.
  • Electroencephalography (EEG): Records electrical activity of the brain via scalp electrodes; used to diagnose epilepsy and assess consciousness.
  • Electromyography (EMG): Records electrical activity of muscles; distinguishes neurogenic from myopathic disorders.
  • Nerve conduction study (NCS): Measures speed and amplitude of nerve impulses; detects demyelination and axonal loss.
  • Lumbar puncture (Spinal tap): Insertion of needle into subarachnoid space (L3-L4 or L4-L5) to obtain CSF for analysis.
  • Biopsy: Tissue sampling for histological diagnosis; includes brain biopsy, nerve biopsy, and muscle biopsy.
  • Nissl stain: Histological stain (cresyl violet) that stains Nissl bodies (RER) in neuronal cell bodies; useful for mapping cytoarchitecture.
  • Golgi stain: Silver-based histological stain that impregnates a small random subset of neurons entirely (cell body, dendrites, axon); revealed neuronal morphology.
  • Myelin stain: Stains myelin sheaths (e.g., Luxol fast blue, Weil stain); used to trace pathways and detect demyelination.
  • Glial fibrillary acidic protein (GFAP): An intermediate filament protein in astrocytes; immunohistochemical stain for GFAP identifies astrocytes and reactive gliosis.

Blood Supply & CSF — Extended

  • Circle of Willis: An anastomotic ring of arteries at the base of the brain formed by the internal carotid arteries, anterior cerebral arteries, anterior communicating artery, posterior communicating arteries, and posterior cerebral arteries. Provides collateral circulation between the anterior and posterior circulations. → vs Cerebral arteries: The three paired major arteries supplying the cerebral hemispheres: anterior (ACA), middle (MCA), and posterior (PCA).
  • Internal carotid artery: One of the two main arterial supplies to the brain; gives rise to the anterior and middle cerebral arteries, plus the ophthalmic artery. Enters the skull through the carotid canal.
  • Vertebral artery: Paired arteries ascending through the transverse foramina of cervical vertebrae C6–C1; the two vertebral arteries unite at the pontomedullary junction to form the basilar artery. Supplies the brainstem, cerebellum, and posterior cerebral arteries.
  • Basilar artery: Formed by the union of the two vertebral arteries; runs along the ventral surface of the pons and gives rise to the posterior cerebral arteries. Occlusion causes "locked-in" syndrome (bilateral ventral pontine infarction).
  • Middle cerebral artery (MCA): The largest cerebral artery; supplies the lateral surface of the frontal, parietal, and temporal lobes. The most common site of ischemic stroke. Occlusion causes contralateral face and arm weakness with aphasia (if dominant hemisphere affected).
  • Anterior cerebral artery (ACA): Supplies the medial surface of the frontal and parietal lobes, including the motor and sensory cortex for the leg and foot. Occlusion causes contralateral leg weakness.
  • Posterior cerebral artery (PCA): Supplies the occipital lobe (primary visual cortex) and inferior temporal lobe. Occlusion causes contralateral homonymous hemianopia.
  • Anterior communicating artery: A short vessel connecting the two anterior cerebral arteries; a common site of berry aneurysms. Rupture may cause subarachnoid hemorrhage.
  • Posterior communicating artery: Connects the internal carotid artery to the posterior cerebral artery on each side. Aneurysm at this location may compress the oculomotor nerve (CN III).
  • Choroid plexus: A highly vascular structure found in the lateral, third, and fourth ventricles; the primary site of cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) production (~500 mL/day). Formed by pia mater, fenestrated capillaries, and ependymal cells.
  • Arachnoid granulations (villi): Projections of the arachnoid mater into the dural venous sinuses (especially the superior sagittal sinus) through which CSF is reabsorbed into the venous blood. → Compare with Choroid plexus: Choroid plexus produces CSF; arachnoid granulations reabsorb it.
  • Superior sagittal sinus: The major dural venous sinus running along the superior margin of the falx cerebri; the primary site of CSF reabsorption via arachnoid granulations.
  • Hydrocephalus: Abnormal accumulation of cerebrospinal fluid in the ventricles, causing ventricular enlargement and increased intracranial pressure. Types: communicating (impaired reabsorption) and non-communicating (obstruction of flow, e.g., cerebral aqueduct stenosis). Treated by surgical shunt placement.
  • Blood-brain barrier (BBB): A highly selective permeability barrier formed by tight junctions between cerebral capillary endothelial cells, a continuous basement membrane, and astrocytic end-feet. Restricts the movement of most substances from the blood into the brain parenchyma. Absent in the circumventricular organs. Example: Only small, lipophilic molecules (oxygen, CO2, ethanol, some drugs) can freely cross the BBB.
  • Dural venous sinus: Venous channels between the periosteal and meningeal layers of the dura mater that drain blood from the brain. Lack valves; rely on gravity and pressure gradients for flow. Major sinuses include superior sagittal, inferior sagittal, straight, transverse, and sigmoid sinuses.

Staining & Imaging Methods

  • Nissl stain (Cresyl violet): A histological stain that labels Nissl bodies (rough endoplasmic reticulum) in neuronal cell bodies; stains basophilically. Used for mapping cytoarchitecture (e.g., cortical layers, Rexed's laminae). Named after Franz Nissl.
  • Golgi stain: A silver-based histological stain that randomly impregnates a small subset of neurons entirely (cell body, dendrites, and axon). Pioneered by Camillo Golgi and used by Santiago Ramón y Cajal to establish the Neuron Doctrine. Example: Revealed dendritic spines and axonal arborizations for the first time.
  • Myelin stain (Luxol fast blue, Weil stain): Stains myelin sheaths blue/black; used to trace white matter pathways and detect demyelination (e.g., multiple sclerosis plaques).
  • GFAP (Glial fibrillary acidic protein): An intermediate filament protein specifically expressed in astrocytes. GFAP immunohistochemistry is used to identify astrocytes in tissue sections and to detect reactive gliosis (upregulation of GFAP) after CNS injury.
  • NeuN (Neuronal Nuclei): A neuron-specific nuclear protein used as an immunohistochemical marker to identify and count neurons in tissue sections.
  • ChAT (Choline acetyltransferase): The enzyme that synthesizes acetylcholine; ChAT immunohistochemistry identifies cholinergic neurons (e.g., motor neurons, autonomic preganglionic neurons). Example: Used to demonstrate sympathetic preganglionic neurons in the intermediolateral horn of the spinal cord.
  • Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI): A non-invasive imaging technique that uses strong magnetic fields and radio waves to produce detailed images of soft tissues. Superior to CT for visualizing white matter, tumors, and posterior fossa structures. Does not use ionizing radiation.
  • Computed tomography (CT): An X-ray-based imaging technique that produces cross-sectional images. Faster and cheaper than MRI; excellent for detecting acute intracranial hemorrhage (appears bright white), skull fractures, and calcifications.
  • Electroencephalography (EEG): A neurophysiological technique that records the brain's electrical activity via scalp electrodes. The primary diagnostic tool for epilepsy and for assessing sleep stages. EEG patterns include alpha (relaxed, 8–13 Hz), beta (alert, 14–30 Hz), theta (fatigued, 4–7 Hz), and delta (deep sleep, 1–3 Hz) waves.
  • Electromyography (EMG): A technique that records electrical activity of muscles via a needle electrode. Used to distinguish neurogenic disorders (e.g., ALS, peripheral neuropathy) from myopathic disorders (e.g., muscular dystrophy).
  • Nerve conduction study (NCS): A test that measures the speed (conduction velocity) and amplitude of electrical impulses traveling along peripheral nerves. Slowed conduction indicates demyelination; reduced amplitude suggests axonal loss.

Sleep & Memory — Extended

  • VLPO (Ventrolateral preoptic nucleus): A group of GABAergic and galaninergic neurons in the anterior hypothalamus that acts as the master "sleep switch." Projects to and inhibits all major awake-promoting centers. Damage causes pathological insomnia.
  • Sleep cycle: Approximately 90-minute cycles of alternating NREM (stages N1 → N2 → N3) and REM sleep. Slow-wave sleep (N3) predominates early in the night; REM sleep predominates later. About 4–6 cycles per night in healthy adults.
  • REM sleep (Rapid Eye Movement sleep): A sleep stage characterized by rapid eye movements, muscle atonia (paralysis except for extraocular muscles and diaphragm), vivid dreaming, and EEG desynchronization resembling wakefulness. Also called "paradoxical sleep."
  • NREM sleep (Non-Rapid Eye Movement sleep): Sleep stages N1 (light sleep), N2 (sleep spindles and K-complexes on EEG), and N3 (slow-wave sleep, delta waves 1–3 Hz). N3 is the deepest and most restorative stage.
  • Semantic memory: Factual knowledge about the world not tied to a specific personal experience (e.g., knowing the capital of France, the meaning of a word, or the function of the hippocampus). Enables understanding without recollection of specific events.
  • Episodic memory: Memory of personally experienced events tied to a specific time and place (e.g., "I visited the Eiffel Tower in June 2019"). Depends on the hippocampus for formation; long-term storage is distributed across the neocortex.
  • Henry Molaison (H.M.): A famous neurological patient who underwent bilateral medial temporal lobe resection (including the hippocampus) in 1953 to treat intractable epilepsy. He developed profound anterograde amnesia (inability to form new episodic memories) while retaining working memory, semantic memory, and procedural learning. This case established that the hippocampus is essential for episodic memory formation but is not the permanent storage site for memories.
  • Sleep switch (flip-flop circuit): A bistable neural circuit in which the VLPO (sleep-promoting) and arousal centers (wake-promoting) mutually inhibit each other, ensuring stable states of sleep and wakefulness rather than intermediate drowsiness. Stabilized by hypocretin/orexin neurons in the lateral hypothalamus.

Language & Motor Planning Disorders

  • Wernicke's aphasia (Receptive/Sensory aphasia): A fluent aphasia caused by damage to the posterior superior temporal gyrus (Brodmann area 22). Speech is fluent and grammatically correct but filled with paraphasias and lacking meaningful content; comprehension is severely impaired; the patient is typically unaware of the deficit.
  • Broca's aphasia (Expressive/Motor aphasia): A non-fluent aphasia caused by damage to the inferior frontal gyrus — pars opercularis and pars triangularis (Brodmann areas 44 and 45). Speech is effortful, agrammatical, and telegraphic; comprehension is relatively preserved; the patient is aware of and frustrated by the deficit. Example: Patient may say "want... water... please" instead of "I would like a glass of water, please."
  • Apraxia: A disorder of motor planning characterized by inability to perform skilled, learned movements on command, despite intact motor function, sensation, and comprehension. Typically caused by parietal or prefrontal cortex damage from stroke, tumor, or trauma. Example: A patient cannot demonstrate how to use a hammer when asked, but can perform the action spontaneously when actually holding a hammer.
  • Orofacial apraxia: Inability to perform voluntary facial movements on verbal command (e.g., "lick your lips," "blow out a match") despite being able to perform these movements spontaneously.
  • Constructional apraxia: Inability to assemble, copy, or draw objects (e.g., copying a simple geometric figure such as a cube or clock face) despite intact visuospatial perception and motor control.

Additional Neurological Disorders

  • Tourette's syndrome: A common neurological disorder characterized by multiple motor and vocal tics — repetitive, involuntary movements and vocalizations. Onset typically in childhood; men affected more than women. Treatment includes dopamine antagonists, behavioral therapy, and social skills training.
  • Wernicke-Korsakoff syndrome (WKS): A neurological disorder caused by severe thiamine (vitamin B1) deficiency, most commonly in chronic alcoholics. Wernicke's encephalopathy: acute phase with the classic triad of confusion, ataxia, and ophthalmoplegia. Korsakoff's syndrome: chronic phase with profound anterograde amnesia and confabulation. Associated with atrophy of the mammillary bodies. Treatment: high-dose thiamine; prognosis poor (~20% mortality within weeks).
  • Infantile cerebral palsy (CP): A group of non-progressive movement disorders caused by damage to the developing brain (prenatal, perinatal, or early postnatal). Four subtypes based on the predominant movement disorder: spastic (~70%, corticospinal tract damage), ataxic (cerebellar damage), athetoid/dyskinetic (basal ganglia damage), and mixed CP. There is no cure; treatment focuses on maximizing function and independence.
  • Substance abuse: The abnormal, compulsive use of a substance leading to impairment in social, occupational, or health functioning. Tolerance: decreased effect at the same dose; withdrawal: physiological symptoms upon cessation. Most addictive drugs act on the brain's dopamine reward pathway (VTA → nucleus accumbens).
  • Rabies: A fatal viral infection transmitted via saliva of infected animals (bats, dogs). The virus travels retrogradely along axons to the CNS, causing encephalitis. Once symptoms appear, it is nearly 100% fatal. In neuroscience research, modified rabies virus is used as a tool for retrograde transsynaptic tracing of neural circuits.
  • Creutzfeldt-Jakob disease (CJD): A rare, rapidly progressive, and fatal neurodegenerative disorder caused by prions — misfolded PrP(Sc) proteins that induce normal PrP(C) proteins to misfold. Sporadic CJD occurs in ~1 in 1,000,000 people. Variant CJD (vCJD) in humans was caused by consumption of BSE (bovine spongiform encephalopathy)-contaminated beef. Diagnosis: clinical symptoms, MRI, CSF analysis (14-3-3 protein), and EEG (periodic sharp wave complexes). Example: Sporadic CJD typically presents with rapidly progressive dementia, myoclonus, and ataxia; death usually occurs within 6–12 months.
  • Limbic system: A term traditionally used to describe a group of forebrain structures (hippocampus, amygdala, cingulate gyrus, septum, mammillary bodies) involved in emotion and memory. Note: This term is now considered outdated and should be avoided because it cannot be precisely defined anatomically and oversimplifies the complex connectivity of these structures. Modern neuroscience describes the functions of individual structures rather than grouping them as a "system." → See Hippocampus, Amygdala
  • Extrapyramidal system: An outdated term traditionally used to describe motor pathways outside the corticospinal (pyramidal) tract, including the basal ganglia, cerebellum, and brainstem motor centers. Note: This term is now considered misleading and should be avoided because it lumps together functionally distinct systems (basal ganglia circuits, cerebellar circuits, brainstem descending pathways) that have fundamentally different roles in motor control. → See Basal ganglia, Cerebellum, Corticospinal tract

Emotion, Reward & Stress

  • Amygdala: Almond-shaped nucleus in the medial temporal lobe; the central hub for emotional processing, especially fear and threat detection. Receives fast sensory input from thalamus and detailed input from sensory cortex. Outputs to hypothalamus (autonomic response), PAG (freezing/escape), and prefrontal cortex (regulation). Example: Bilateral amygdala damage impairs fear conditioning and recognition of fearful facial expressions (Urbach-Wiethe disease).
  • Periaqueductal gray (PAG): A region of grey matter surrounding the cerebral aqueduct in the midbrain; mediates defensive behaviors including freezing and fight-or-flight responses. Receives direct input from the amygdala and hypothalamus. Example: Electrical stimulation of dorsal PAG produces panic-like escape behavior; ventral PAG stimulation produces freezing.
  • Mesolimbic dopamine pathway: The core reward circuit of the brain: dopaminergic neurons in the ventral tegmental area (VTA) project to the nucleus accumbens, prefrontal cortex, and amygdala. Dopamine release in the accumbens signals reward prediction error — when an outcome is better than expected. All major addictive substances increase dopamine signaling in this pathway. → See Ventral tegmental area, Nucleus accumbens
  • Ventral tegmental area (VTA): A group of dopaminergic neurons in the midbrain medial to the substantia nigra; the origin of the mesolimbic and mesocortical dopamine pathways. VTA → accumbens projection is the basis of reward, motivation, and addiction.
  • Nucleus accumbens: The ventral part of the striatum, located where the caudate nucleus and putamen merge; receives dense dopaminergic input from the VTA. Central to the reward system, motivation, and reinforcement learning. Example: Increased dopamine release in the accumbens occurs in response to food, sex, money, and all addictive drugs.
  • Tolerance: Decreased response to a drug after repeated use, requiring higher doses to achieve the same effect. Mediated by downregulation of postsynaptic receptors and adaptive changes in intracellular signaling cascades.
  • Withdrawal: Physiological and psychological symptoms that occur when substance use is discontinued after physical dependence has developed. Symptoms are typically opposite to the drug's acute effects (e.g., opioid withdrawal causes dysphoria, anxiety, pain sensitivity — opposite to opioid euphoria and analgesia).
  • HPA axis (Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Adrenal axis): The body's primary stress response system. Paraventricular nucleus of hypothalamus → CRH → anterior pituitary → ACTH → adrenal cortex → cortisol. Cortisol mobilizes energy, suppresses non-essential functions, and provides negative feedback via hippocampal and hypothalamic glucocorticoid receptors. Example: Chronic HPA activation in major depression is associated with elevated cortisol, reduced hippocampal volume, and impaired negative feedback (dexamethasone non-suppression).
  • Cortisol: The primary glucocorticoid hormone in humans; released by the adrenal cortex in response to ACTH. Mobilizes glucose, suppresses immune function, and modulates brain function. Chronically elevated cortisol is neurotoxic to hippocampal neurons. → Compare with Adrenaline (epinephrine): Adrenaline is released from the adrenal medulla (sympathetic activation) and acts within seconds; cortisol acts over minutes to hours via gene transcription.
  • CRH (Corticotropin-releasing hormone): A peptide hormone released by the paraventricular nucleus of the hypothalamus that stimulates ACTH release from the anterior pituitary. Also acts as a neuromodulator in the amygdala (promoting anxiety-like behaviors) and other brain regions.
  • Anterior cingulate cortex (ACC): A region of the medial prefrontal cortex involved in error detection, conflict monitoring, emotional salience, and empathy. The dorsal ACC is more cognitive (error monitoring); the ventral ACC (subgenual) is more emotional. Example: Subgenual ACC hyperactivity is associated with major depression; deep brain stimulation of this region has shown antidepressant effects.
  • Ventromedial prefrontal cortex (vmPFC): The ventral and medial portion of the prefrontal cortex; critical for evaluating the emotional and motivational value of choices, regulating emotional responses, and decision-making under uncertainty. Damage impairs social and emotional decision-making (e.g., Phineas Gage, the famous case of vmPFC damage causing personality change).
  • Insula (Insular cortex): A region of cortex buried within the lateral sulcus; processes interoception (sensation of internal body states), disgust, empathy for others' pain, and emotional awareness. The anterior insula is activated during subjective feelings and is part of the "salience network." Example: The insula is thought to mediate the conscious urge to smoke in nicotine addiction; damage to the insula can abruptly eliminate smoking desire.

Development, Plasticity & Aging

  • Synaptic pruning: The activity-dependent elimination of excess synapses during development and adolescence. Frequently used synapses are strengthened and retained; unused synapses are removed. This process refines neural circuits and is critical for efficient brain function. Example: Pruning in the prefrontal cortex continues through adolescence, coinciding with improvements in impulse control and decision-making. "Use it or lose it."
  • Critical period: A restricted developmental time window during which the brain is especially sensitive to specific types of experience. After the critical period closes, the same experience has much less (or no) effect on circuit organization. Example: Hubel and Wiesel (1960s) showed that covering one eye in kittens during the visual critical period (first 3 months) permanently impaired vision in that eye; the same manipulation in adult cats had no effect. → Compare with Sensitive period: A broader, more flexible window when the brain is most receptive to certain experiences, but learning can still occur later.
  • Cognitive reserve: The brain's ability to maintain function despite age-related changes or pathology (e.g., Alzheimer's disease). Built through higher education, bilingualism, complex mental activity, physical exercise, and social engagement throughout life. Explains why some individuals with significant Alzheimer's pathology show minimal cognitive impairment — their brains have developed redundant networks and alternative strategies.
  • Adult neurogenesis: The generation of new neurons in the adult brain, occurring primarily in the hippocampal dentate gyrus and subventricular zone (→ olfactory bulb). New hippocampal neurons are important for pattern separation and cognitive flexibility. Enhanced by exercise, enriched environment, learning, and certain antidepressants (SSRIs); impaired by chronic stress, sleep deprivation, aging, and alcohol abuse.
  • BDNF (Brain-Derived Neurotrophic Factor): A protein that supports the survival, growth, and differentiation of neurons; promotes synaptic plasticity and is critical for long-term memory formation. Exercise increases BDNF expression in the hippocampus. Example: The Val66Met polymorphism in the BDNF gene is associated with reduced hippocampal function and increased risk of depression and anxiety disorders.
  • Hebbian plasticity / "Fire together, wire together": The principle that synaptic strength increases when pre- and postsynaptic neurons are repeatedly co-active. The cellular basis of learning and memory, formalized by Donald Hebb in 1949. The opposite — "out of sync, lose the link" — underlies synaptic pruning. Example: Long-term potentiation (LTP), the cellular correlate of memory, is a Hebbian mechanism requiring coincident pre- and postsynaptic activity at glutamatergic synapses.
  • Default mode network (DMN): A set of brain regions (medial prefrontal cortex, posterior cingulate cortex, angular gyrus, medial temporal lobe) that are active during rest, self-referential thought, mind-wandering, and autobiographical memory. The DMN is deactivated during goal-directed tasks. Altered DMN connectivity is implicated in Alzheimer's disease, depression, and autism.

Neurotransmitters & Signaling

  • Acetylcholine (ACh): Neurotransmitter at neuromuscular junctions, in autonomic ganglia, and in parasympathetic postganglionic synapses. Degraded by acetylcholinesterase.
  • Dopamine: Catecholamine neurotransmitter produced in substantia nigra and ventral tegmental area; critical for movement (nigrostriatal pathway) and reward (mesolimbic pathway).
  • Serotonin (5-HT): Neurotransmitter produced in raphe nuclei of brainstem; regulates mood, sleep, and appetite.
  • Norepinephrine (Noradrenaline): Catecholamine neurotransmitter produced in locus coeruleus; involved in arousal, attention, and sympathetic function.
  • GABA (Gamma-aminobutyric acid): The main inhibitory neurotransmitter in the CNS.
  • Glutamate: The main excitatory neurotransmitter in the CNS.
  • Glycine: Inhibitory neurotransmitter in spinal cord and brainstem.
  • Neuropeptide: A peptide used as a neurotransmitter or neuromodulator (e.g., substance P, endorphins, oxytocin, vasopressin).
  • Neurotrophic factor: A protein that supports neuron survival, growth, and differentiation (e.g., NGF, BDNF, GDNF).
